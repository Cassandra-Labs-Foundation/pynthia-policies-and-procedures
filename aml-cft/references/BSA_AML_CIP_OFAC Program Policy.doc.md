**BSA/AML/CIP/OFAC Program**

**PURPOSE**

The **Bank Secrecy Act (BSA)** was designed to fight drug trafficking, money laundering, terrorist financing, and other illicit financial transactions.  Congress enacted the BSA to help prevent banks and other financial service providers from being used as intermediaries for, or being used to hide the transfer or deposit of money derived from, criminal activity.  Suspicious activity reporting forms the cornerstone of the BSA reporting system and is incorporated into a financial institution’s **Anti-Money Laundering (AML)** program.  The passage of the USA PATRIOT Act after the terrorist attacks required that banks have a written **Customer Identification Program (CIP)** that is incorporated into the BSA/AML program.  The CIP is intended to enable the bank to form a reasonable belief that it knows the true identity of each customer.  Finally, the **Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC)** administers and enforces economic and trade sanctions based on U.S. foreign policy and national security goals against entities such as targeted foreign countries, terrorists, international narcotics traffickers, and those engaged in activities related to the proliferations of weapons of mass destruction.

Be it resolved that this is the policy of Bank \_\_\_ (Bank \_\_\_ or Bank) to maintain maximum compliance with the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA), Anti-Money Laundering (AML) efforts, the Customer Identification Program (CIP) rules, and the Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) requirements under Federal, FDIC, and Internal Revenue Service regulations.  This is referred to as the “BSA program.” 

**BSA COMPLIANCE PROGRAM REQUIREMENTS**

The Federal banking agencies have adopted rules that require each bank to develop and provide for the continued administration of a compliance program that, at a minimum, provides for:

1. designation of an individual to be responsible for the coordination and monitoring of daily compliance.  
2. periodic training to all appropriate personnel, which will be tailored to the person’s specific responsibilities for the BSA.  
3. independent testing for compliance concerning the BSA program every 12 to 18 months by qualified external auditors.  
4. a system of internal controls to assure ongoing compliance with the requirements of BSA, detecting and preventing money laundering, and reporting suspicious activities.

**COMPLIANCE RESPONSIBILITIES**

The Board of Directors will be ultimately responsible for the financial institution’s BSA compliance (certain responsibilities may be delegated to the Compliance Committee), as follows:

* Reviewing and approving the written BSA/AML/CIP/OFAC compliance program at least annually.  
* Understanding the importance of BSA regulatory requirements, ramifications of noncompliance, and risks posed to the Bank.  
* Appointing a qualified individual as the BSA Officer.  
* Ensuring that the BSA Officer has sufficient authority and resources (monetary, physical and personnel) to administer an effective risk-based BSA compliance program.  
* Reviewing audits and training programs as needed.  
* Reviewing recommendations regarding the BSA compliance program made by the BSA Officer, external auditors, or examiners.  
* Maintaining awareness of the Bank’s BSA program efforts through review of periodic reports by the BSA Officer.  
* Keeping BSA/AML matters discussed at Board meetings confidential such as suspicious activity reporting.

The Board of Directors has designated \_\_\_ as the BSA Officer who is responsible for coordinating and monitoring day-to-day compliance and managing all aspects of the BSA and OFAC programs. The BSA Officer (or other designated employees) will be responsible for the following concerning compliance with the BSA program (not all-inclusive list):

* Developing and maintaining BSA/AML/CIP/OFAC policies and procedures.  
* Monitoring, supervising, coordinating, and documenting the BSA program, including the timely filing of all required BSA reports; making recommendations when and where appropriate to enhance the BSA program.  
* Maintaining proficient knowledge of the regulations and guidance, including but not limited to, the BSA, USA Patriot Act, and OFAC.  
* Researching regulatory issues and responding to BSA-related compliance questions from Bank personnel.  
* Disseminating BSA-related information to appropriate staff and providing assistance where necessary.  
* Providing periodic reports to the Board of Directors regarding BSA activities, including suspicious activity reporting.  
* Reviewing OFAC and Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) reports, including 314(a) requests, and assuring compliance with current regulations.  
* Monitoring the Currency Transaction Reporting (CTR) system and ensuring timely filing of accurate reports.  
* Reviewing various monetary instrument, wire transfer, large transaction, kiting suspect, and similar reports for any type of suspicious activity.  
* Monitoring the customer base for suspicious activity and providing Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) for a second review and to the Board and appropriate regulatory agencies.  
* Ensuring compliance with the Customer Identification Program (CIP) requirements and internal procedures.  
* Conducting a risk assessment of each customer and monitoring high-risk and moderate-risk customers with documented reviews of activity levels.  
* Performing a risk assessment of the Bank’s customer base, products and services, geographic location, and operations every 12 to 18 months; performing a similar OFAC risk assessment.  
* Providing and/or coordinating periodic training to existing and new employees and Board members.  
* Reviewing any additions or deletions from the BSA exemption list and completing reports in this area where necessary.  
* Reviewing and implementing, where feasible, any anti-money laundering software.  
* Serving as liaison between the Bank, the FDIC, the State, and external auditors for examinations and audits.    
* Providing all BSA examination and audit reports to the Compliance Committee, including  responses and corrective actions.  
* Maintaining records of all BSA reporting for a five-year period.

**BSA TRAINING**

A training program will be in effect to assure all applicable personnel are properly trained in BSA responsibilities appropriate to their area.  The BSA/AML/CIP/OFAC Employee Manual (also referred to as BSA Employee Manual) will be utilized during training, including orientation of new employees, where applicable.  In addition, the BSA Officer and/or BSA Specialist will provide training to employees and Board members on a periodic basis.  On-line BSA-related training also may be required by certain employees, including new employees and refresher courses for existing employees.  The BSA Officer, with the assistance of Human Resources, will maintain a record of individuals trained, dates of training, and a copy of any materials presented.  The BSA Officer, and any designated BSA staff, shall periodically attend educational programs related to BSA/AML/CIP/OFAC compliance.

**INDEPENDENT TESTING**

The Bank will approve a qualified external auditor to be responsible for conducting an independent risk-based evaluation for compliance with the BSA/AML/CIP/OFAC requirements every 12 to 18 months.  The audit should be done utilizing risk-based procedures that cover all of the Bank’s activities.  At a minimum, testing should be performed using the Federal Financial Institutions Examination Council (FFIEC) BSA/AML Examination Manual guidelines.  A written report of the audit results and steps taken to remedy any deficiencies shall be reported to the Compliance Committee of the Board.

In addition, the BSA Officer may perform internal reviews of various BSA-related areas to determine if staff are adhering to the procedures contained in the BSA Employee Manual and BSA Operations Procedures Manual.  Any results of these BSA reviews will be provided to the Compliance Committee. All audit/examination/internal review findings and recommendations will be tracked on a spreadsheet to ensure an effective system is in place for correction of items.

**INTERNAL CONTROLS**

The Bank shall institute a system of internal controls to ensure ongoing compliance that includes, but is not limited to:

* Performing a BSA and OFAC risk assessment by identifying the financial institution’s operations (products, services, customers, and geographic locations) more vulnerable to abuse by money launderers and criminals; providing for periodic updates to the Bank’s risk profile (generally every 12 – 18 months); and providing for a BSA/AML compliance program tailored to manage risks.  
* Incorporating a review of BSA/OFAC risks, when necessary, for any new products or services introduced by the Bank.  
* Informing the Board of Directors and senior management of BSA activities every other month that incorporates the following (subject to change):  
  * Suspicious Activity Report(s) – brief description (if insider transaction, Board will be notified at next scheduled monthly meeting or sooner)  
  * Do Not File Report – brief description of activity not considered SAR reportable  
  * Currency Transaction Reports – number filed during review period  
  * Foreign Wires – number and dollar amount   
  * High Risk Accounts – number reported  
  * BSA Training – brief summary of training  
  * BSA Audits – brief summary of any recent BSA-related audits  
  * BSA Regulatory Issues – description of proposed, new or revised regulations or other guidance from regulatory agencies  
  * Other BSA Issues – if needed, will be described  
* Providing for program continuity despite changes in management or employee composition or structure.  
* Meeting all regulatory recordkeeping and reporting requirements, meeting recommendations for BSA/AML compliance, and providing for timely updates in response to changes in regulations.  
* Implementing risk-based customer due diligence (CDD) policies, procedures, and processes.  
* Providing sufficient controls, reviews, separation of duties, and monitoring systems for timely detection and reporting of suspicious activity and accurately filing all required reports including SARs, CTRs, and CTR exemptions.  
* Providing for adequate supervision of employees that handle currency transactions, monitoring CIP procedural requirements, completing reports, granting exemptions, monitoring for suspicious activity, or engaging in any other activity covered by the BSA and its implementing regulations.

**BSA AND OFAC REGULATORY REQUIREMENTS**

The Bank recognizes BSA and OFAC compliance is a safety and soundness issue with several key requirements.  In accordance with these requirements, it is the policy of the Bank to:

* Report currency transactions exceeding $10,000 to the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN).  
* Grant exemptions to currency transaction reporting, where appropriate.  
* Obtain certain information from customers and non-customers who purchase monetary instruments for cash in amounts from $3,000 to $10,000.  
* Collect and retain certain information in connection with funds transfers of $3,000 or more.  
* Retain certain records for a period of five years.  
* Establish a risk-based Customer Identification Program.  
* Institute a formal risk-based Anti-Money Laundering Program.  
* Report suspicious activities to the Board of Directors and appropriate authorities.  
* Review government lists.  
* Communicate with third parties through information sharing covered under the 314(a) and 314(b) regulatory requirements.  
* Ensure accountholders and certain types of transactions are verified for OFAC compliance and block or prohibit transactions when required per risk-based procedures.  
* Report all blockings to OFAC when required.

Specific procedures regarding compliance with the aforementioned BSA and OFAC requirements are separately outlined in the written BSA/AML/CIP/OFAC Compliance Program Employee Manual and the BSA Operations Procedures Manual.  Employees shall be aware of their responsibilities and accountability for compliance with all BSA and OFAC-related requirements, which are described in the two aforementioned manuals.

**ANTI-MONEY LAUNDERING PROGRAM**

The objectives of the Anti-Money Laundering program are to:

* Facilitate the Bank’s compliance with the BSA and other related laws and regulations, including filing SARs as required.  
* Ensure full compliance with the Bank’s established Customer Identification Program.  
* Ensure employees are knowledgeable regarding the money laundering process and suspicious transactions.  
* Facilitate safe and sound banking practices.  
* Decrease the likelihood that the institution will become a victim of illegal activities perpetrated by its customers.  
* Protect the good name and reputation of BOCF.

It is the policy of the Bank \_\_\_ to implement policies and procedures that assist in detecting and preventing money laundering, terrorist financing, or other illegal activities conducted through transactions at the Bank.   In accordance with Section 352 of the USA PATRIOT Act and 31 CFR 1020.210, BOCF has established formal procedures to minimize exposure to transactions, compliance, and reputation risks associated with money laundering. The Bank makes risk-based assessments to identify potential risks associated with products and services offered, the customers served, the Bank’s operations, and the geographic locations of the offices.  To manage risk, the AML procedures include:

* Account opening controls in accordance with the Customer Identification Program.  
* Customer due diligence (CDD) and enhanced due diligence (EDD) efforts.  
* Screening of government lists, when applicable.  
* Daily, weekly, and monthly reviews of various reports such as CTR large transaction reports, kiting suspect reports, monetary instrument logs, wire logs, automated clearinghouse (ACH) reports, and suspicious tracking reports.  
* Monitoring and reporting of suspicious activity.

All employees shall be provided information as to the types of suspicious activity that could occur within their operational positions.  Procedures have been developed to report suspicious activity, when warranted, to the BSA Officer (or designated staff).  A decision will be made by the BSA Department as to whether a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) will be filed; the SAR is prepared as necessary by the BSA Officer (or designated staff); forwarded for confirmation through a second review process; sent in a timely manner to the appropriate Federal agency and state and local authorities (if applicable); and reported to the Board at the next scheduled BSA Activity report (insider SAR transactions to be reported at next scheduled Board meeting if not sooner).  If a SAR is not required, files will be maintained for Do Not File (DNF) activity. A BSA Committee structure is in place to make a determination on whether to keep open or close accounts for subjects who have generally had three consecutive SARs filed.  All Board members and employees are trained on the importance of maintaining the confidentiality of SAR information.  Specific SAR-related procedures are described in the BSA Employee Manual and the BSA Operations Procedures Manual.

**CUSTOMER IDENTIFICATION PROGRAM**

It is the policy of the Bank \_\_\_ to form a reasonable belief that it knows the true identity of each customer through risk-based procedures.  As required by Section 326 of the USA PATRIOT Act and 31 CFR 1020.220, the BOCF has implemented a Customer Identification Program, consisting of procedures for:

* Verifying the identity of any customer opening an account, to the extent reasonable and practicable.  
* Maintaining records of the information used to verify the customer’s identity, including name, address, date of birth (for individuals), and taxpayer identification number for U.S. persons and number and country of issuance of a government-issued document for non-U.S. persons.  
* Determining if the customer appears on any government list of known or suspected terrorists or terrorist organizations for purposes of Section 326 provided to the Bank by any government agency.   
* Providing adequate customer notice that the Bank is requesting information to verify their identities.  
* Determining whether a SAR should be filed (see separate Anti-Money Laundering section of this policy).

In compliance with the CIP rules, the Bank will take appropriate steps to ensure the accuracy of the customer information collected.  The Bank uses documentary verification (e.g., driver’s license) and non-documentary verification (e.g., credit report) methods to verify customer information. In addition, risk-based procedures have been established for customer due diligence (CDD) and enhanced due diligence (EDD) to differentiate between lower risk and higher risk customers.  

Due to the high risk nature of the entity, it is the policy of the Bank not to open accounts for the following:

* Politically Exposed Person (PEP) – includes a current or former senior foreign political figure, their immediate family, and any close associates.  
* Money Services Business (MSB) – includes currency dealers or exchangers; check cashers; issuers of traveler’s checks, money orders, or prepaid access cards; sellers or redeemers of traveler’s checks, money orders, or prepaid access cards; or money transmitters that meet the threshold requirement of transactions in an amount greater than $1,000 for any person on any day in one or more transactions (money transmitters do not have this threshold and are considered a MSB).  
* Unlawful Internet Gambling Accounts – includes commercial customers that may use an account for unlawful gambling activities under federal, state, and Regulation GG laws.  
* Payment Processor Relationships – includes deposit customers who may process payments for merchant clients (certain merchants, such as telemarketing and online, may pose a greater money laundering and fraud risk).  
* Privately Owned ATM Entities – includes businesses who may have privately owned ATMs in locations such as convenience stores, bars, restaurants, or check cashing establishments.  
* Marijuana\-Related Business – includes businesses who deal with the production, distribution or sale of either medical marijuana or recreational marijuana.  
* Virtual Currency Entity – includes businesses who serves as an administrator or exchanger that accepts and transmits convertible virual currency or buys or sells convertible virtual currency.

In addition, the Bank will not generally accept deposits through “deposit brokers.”  Use of deposit brokers can present additional safety and soundness and BSA/AML/OFAC risk to the Bank.  Enhanced procedures have been instituted to identify and minimize opening the aforementioned high-risk accounts.

Complete details of internal CIP procedures are included in the BSA Employee Manual and the BSA Operations Procedures Manual.

**INFORMATION SHARING**

It is the policy of Bank \_\_\_ to comply with all provisions regarding the USA PATRIOT Act’s Section 314(a) process required by FinCEN.  This process involves law enforcement agencies funneling requests through FinCEN, who in turn communicate with financial institutions to determine whether individuals and entities suspected of engaging in money laundering or terrorist financing activities are accountholders of the financial institution upon a records search.  The BSA Officer, or designated employee, will be responsible for searching records within 14 calendar days of each 314(a) request and reporting any positive matches.

It is the policy of Bank \_\_\_ to comply with regulations involving Section 314(b) of the USA PATRIOT Act, when applicable.  The regulation establishes a mechanism where financial institutions are allowed to register to share information with other financial institutions in order to identify and report activities that they believe may relate to terrorist activity or money laundering.  The Bank has registered with FinCEN and is allowed to share information with other institutions who also are registered.  In order to continue with Section 314(b) information sharing, the Bank will be required to renew the certification annually.

More comprehensive procedures are referenced in the BSA Operations Procedures Manual.

**OFFICE OF FOREIGN ASSETS CONTROL (OFAC) PROGRAM**

In general, OFAC regulations:

* Require the blocking or freezing of accounts and other assets of specified countries, entities, and individuals.  
* Prohibit unlicensed trade and financial transactions with specified countries, entities, and individuals. 

The OFAC list (Specially Designated Nationals) is updated frequently and readily available to financial institutions.  New accounts will be compared with the OFAC list prior to being opened or shortly thereafter, and wire transactions will be screened for OFAC compliance prior to transmission.  In addition, risk-based procedures will be established to review certain types of transactions (e.g., cashing on-us checks) against the OFAC list. 

It is the policy of Bank \_\_\_ to comply with all Federal laws, regulations, and orders regarding doing business with, maintaining accounts for, or handling transactions or monetary transfers for individuals or entities on the OFAC list and targeted countries.  If it is found that the Bank has an account or a customer of the Bank is shown on the OFAC list, all accounts of such customer shall be blocked and held in accordance with instructions from the U.S. Treasury Department.

To comply with OFAC requirements, the Bank has made risk-based assessments and implemented procedures consisting of:

* Maintaining the list of prohibited countries, entities, and individuals (OFAC list).  
* Comparing new accounts  and certain types of transactions to the OFAC list.  
* Comparing incoming and outgoing funds transfers to the OFAC list.  
* Reviewing international automated clearinghouse transactions (IATs) to the OFAC list.  
* Performing an OFAC review of new employees and new vendors for accounts payable transactions.  
* Monitoring transactions for possible prohibited activity.  
* Comparing the Bank’s entire customer database to the OFAC list on a monthly basis.  
* Having a process for determining valid matches versus false hits.  
* Having a process for blocking accounts and rejecting transactions, where applicable, and for notifying OFAC and the customer.

Specific procedures are detailed separately in the BSA Employee Manual and the BSA Operations Manual.

**PENALTIES**

Community banks are at a greater risk today of being used by money launderers and terrorists, as larger financial institutions and those in locations more likely to be used by money launderers become more vigilant.  Should terrorists or money launderers use a community bank, the consequences could be severe and include the following:

* Bank employees and banks can be fined and employees imprisoned.  
* Any property involved or traceable to the proceeds of criminal activity, including loan collateral and bank accounts, may be subject to forfeiture.  
* Bank may risk losing charter.  
* Bank employee termination of employment.  
* Bank employee may be removed and barred from banking.  
* Potential supervisory enforcement action by regulatory agency(ies), including civil money penalties and cease-and-desist orders.  
* Bad media press and decline in public confidence.  
* Limitations on bank applications such as expansion of branches or acquisitions.

It is important to note that criminal and civil penalties can be imposed on Bank personnel or the institution for willfully and knowingly assisting in money laundering or structuring transactions to avoid CTR reporting.  A Bank employee could be subject to a criminal fine of up to $250,000 or five years in prison, or both.  A person who commits such a violation while violating another U.S. law, or engaging in a pattern of criminal activity, is subject to a fine of up to $500,000 or ten years in prison, or both.  A bank that violates certain BSA provisions faces criminal money penalties up to the greater of $1 million or twice the value of the transaction.

Other specific penalties include:

* Failure to maintain compliance procedures may cause the imposition of civil penalties of up to a maximum of $1,000 per day for each day of non-compliance.  *This includes procedures related to customer identification.*  
* A penalty of not more than $500 per violation can be imposed if the Bank violates the recordkeeping or reporting requirements of the BSA.  Willful violations may cause penalties in an amount equivalent to that of the transaction (up to $100,000) or $25,000, whichever is greater.  *This includes filing a CTR, maintaining monetary instrument logs, wire transfer logs, etc.*  
* If a required CTR is not filed timely, a $10,000 per day civil money penalty may be imposed until it is filed.

There are both civil and criminal penalties for OFAC violations.  Depending on the sanctions program, criminal penalties can include fines ranging from $50,000 to $10 million and imprisonment ranging from 10 to 30 years for willful violations.  Civil penalties range from $250,000 or twice the amount of each underlying transaction to $1,075,000 for each violation.  The majority of fines result from a bank’s failure to block illicit transfers (like wires) when there was a reference to a targeted country or Specially Designated Nationals (SDNs).  The Bank also could face the risk of the collateral for loan transactions being subject to forfeiture if the individual or entity is identified as being on the OFAC SDN list.
