## Overview {#overview}

Pynthia Credit Union, a California-chartered federally insured credit union, operates nationwide through member-facing digital channels with operational personnel located in California, South Carolina, Texas, and Porto, Portugal. This Business Continuity Plan Policy establishes the credit union's commitment to withstanding, responding to, and recovering from disruptive events affecting its facilities, systems, vendors, data, and people, regardless of where those events originate. The program is Board-approved and risk-based, guided by four priorities in the order they apply: protecting human safety first, preserving continuity of critical member-facing services, executing prudent and orderly recovery within defined objectives, and extracting organizational learning from every significant incident. Because Pynthia serves members through electronic payment channels — including ACH, wire transfers, debit and ATM networks, mobile banking, and real-time payment services — those channels are designated the highest-priority critical services in the continuity program. The credit union's distinctive risk profile combines the hazard diversity of four geographically dispersed operating locations, the cross-border regulatory complexity of maintaining an office in Portugal subject to European data-protection and employment law, and the systemic dependency risks common to digital-first financial institutions. This document presents the program in narrative form for regulators and oversight parties; the companion structured policy contains the complete control specifications, timing requirements, and system-behavior definitions.

## Regulatory Framework {#regulatory-framework}

Pynthia Credit Union's Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery program is grounded in the federal regulatory framework applicable to federally insured credit unions and draws on supervisory best practices published by the federal financial regulators.

The foundation of the program is NCUA's records preservation and catastrophic act preparedness rules, which require every federally insured credit union to maintain a documented, tested plan for preserving vital records and continuing critical operations following a catastrophic event. These rules demand not only that the credit union identify and protect the records necessary to reconstruct member accounts and obligations, but also that it periodically test its recovery capability and demonstrate that key functions can be restored within defined timeframes. Pynthia's continuity program treats these requirements as the floor for every control from governance through post-incident learning.

NCUA's information security program requirements, aligned with the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act's safeguards framework, impose complementary obligations. The credit union must maintain an information security program that includes risk identification and assessment, implementation of appropriate safeguards, ongoing monitoring, incident response procedures, and oversight of service providers that have access to member information. The incident response provisions in particular require a documented program for detecting security events, containing and eradicating threats, recovering affected systems and data, and determining whether regulatory notification and member notice are required. NCUA's companion requirements on the timing of reportable cyber incident notifications to the agency set specific deadlines that the program operationalizes across multiple controls.

Board responsibilities under NCUA's governance rules establish that the Board of Directors must approve key credit union policies and retain accountability for the continuity program even when day-to-day execution is delegated to management. These rules shape the governance architecture of Pynthia's program, including the requirement for annual Board approval of the policy, quarterly oversight of the Incident Management Team roster, and Board-level reporting after each enterprise exercise.

The FFIEC Business Continuity Management Booklet provides supervisory guidance that, while not binding regulation, reflects examiner expectations across the federal financial regulatory agencies. The credit union's program architecture — including the business impact analysis, threat assessment by geography, tiered backup and recovery objectives, alternate-site planning, and post-incident review discipline — aligns with the FFIEC booklet's framework and is designed to withstand NCUA examination scrutiny.

For the Portugal office, the program accounts for European Union data protection law and employment law as operational constraints that affect how the credit union conducts incident response, routes member data to alternate infrastructure, and activates emergency staffing measures. These are not the primary regulatory drivers of the policy but represent material compliance considerations that the program must reflect in the relevant controls.

## Governance and Roles {#governance-and-roles}

Effective business continuity begins with clear accountability at every level of the organization, and Pynthia's program places that accountability explicitly on the Board and senior management rather than treating it as a purely operational matter. The Board of Directors approves the BCP/DR policy at least annually, retaining ultimate oversight even as it delegates day-to-day program execution to the Chief Compliance Officer and the Business Continuity Manager. This governance structure reflects the Board's obligations under applicable credit union regulation and ensures that the continuity program receives the leadership attention necessary to remain current with the credit union's evolving risk profile and operational footprint.

At the management level, the program is built around a named Incident Management Team whose roster is actively maintained and verified on a quarterly basis. Each position on the IMT has a documented succession path so that the team can function effectively even if named individuals are unavailable during a disruption. The living policy document, with its named owners and clearly assigned responsibilities, serves as the authoritative reference for who is accountable for each element of the program. Policy approval, roster updates, and changes to the governance structure are access-controlled so that only authorized roles can make modifications, providing an auditable record of program stewardship.

## Risk Assessment — Hazards by Region {#risk-assessment-hazards-by-region}

Pynthia's risk assessment begins with the recognition that the credit union's four operating locations face materially different natural hazard profiles that must each be reflected in the continuity program. California presents the combination of wildfire and smoke events, seismic activity, power grid outages including public safety power shutoffs, and drought conditions that can compound operational disruption. South Carolina carries hurricane and tropical storm risk alongside flooding and tornado exposure. Texas adds tornado, severe storm, and extreme heat profiles, as well as the winter storm and grid-outage risk that has proven consequential in recent years. The Porto, Portugal office faces flood risk, seismic activity, extreme heat, and the possibility of disruptive changes to the European regulatory environment. All four locations share common cyber threat, pandemic, and key-person dependency risks that must be managed on an enterprise basis.

The program maintains a formal threat register that scores each identified hazard by both likelihood and potential impact, tagged to the specific geography where it applies. This register is the foundation for the business impact analysis and recovery planning, ensuring that resources and recovery priorities reflect actual risk exposure rather than generic assumptions. The register is refreshed at least once per year and updated on an interim basis whenever a material operational or environmental change warrants it — for example, a significant expansion of the Portugal operation or a major shift in the cyber threat landscape would trigger an out-of-cycle update.

For the Portugal office specifically, the threat register addresses two regulatory hazard categories that do not apply in the same form to the domestic locations: the constraints on cross-border personal data transfers under European data protection law, and the local labor-law requirements that govern how the credit union can direct or redeploy employees during an emergency. These are not abstract risks; they affect the practical availability of response options during an incident and must be factored into alternate-site planning, staffing activations, and incident response coordination. The credit union works with local legal counsel to keep these assessments current as the European regulatory environment evolves.

## Business Impact Analysis {#business-impact-analysis}

The business impact analysis is the mechanism through which the credit union translates its hazard assessment into concrete recovery priorities. Every service and operational capability in scope is cataloged, ranked by its member impact and regulatory dependency, and assigned a recovery sequence. Vital records required to reconstruct member accounts and satisfy legal obligations are identified separately and linked to backup and preservation controls. Recovery time and recovery point objectives are set for each tier of the service catalog, creating the targets that the backup, alternate-site, and IT-response controls are designed to meet.

Electronic payment channels occupy the highest priority tier in the BIA by policy, reflecting their direct member impact, regulatory scrutiny, and the reputational consequences of extended unavailability. The BIA is updated in full at least once per year and is certified on a quarterly basis to confirm that the recovery priorities and objectives remain accurate in light of any changes to the credit union's services, technology, or dependencies. When a new service is onboarded before a full BIA cycle can be completed, it receives a provisional criticality tier that triggers a follow-up review, ensuring that no material new capability enters production without some continuity assessment. The authoritative detail on payment-channel controls lives in the Electronic Payment Systems Policy; the BIA service catalog is reconciled with that policy each cycle.

## Training, Testing, and Exercises {#training-testing-and-exercises}

A continuity plan that is never tested is not a reliable continuity plan, and Pynthia's program requires at least one enterprise-level exercise each year to validate that the plan works in practice and to identify gaps before they become failures during an actual event. The annual exercise may take the form of an orientation exercise, a facilitated tabletop scenario, or a functional exercise that tests actual system and communication capabilities; the choice of format is made based on the program's maturity, recent incident history, and identified gaps from prior exercises.

Exercise results feed directly into a corrective action plan that tracks each identified gap from identification through to remediation and verification. Any element that fails during an exercise generates a remediation task with an assigned owner and a due date, so that gaps do not simply get noted and forgotten. The Board receives a report on exercise results within thirty days of each exercise, maintaining the Board-level visibility into program effectiveness that governance rules require. Over time, the exercise history and the corrective action record together demonstrate the program's continuous improvement and provide the documentation that examiners expect to see.

## Monitoring, Detection, and Severity {#monitoring-detection-and-severity}

Effective response to any disruptive event depends first on detecting it quickly and characterizing it accurately. Pynthia maintains a central on-call monitoring capability with integrated feeds from weather services, cyber threat intelligence sources, and vendor monitoring channels, all converging on a severity matrix that classifies events from the most severe to routine monitoring signals. The severity matrix provides consistent, objective criteria for escalation so that the organization's response is proportionate and predictable.

For the most severe events — those capable of materially disrupting critical services or posing an immediate threat to member data — the program requires an Incident Commander to be assigned within five minutes of detection and initial communications to be issued within fifteen minutes. These tight time standards reflect the reality that delays in the first minutes of a significant incident compound into much larger consequences, both operationally and in terms of regulatory notification obligations. Lower-severity events are handled through the standard triage process without mandatory Incident Commander escalation, keeping the organization's most intensive response resources focused on situations that genuinely require them.

## Incident Declaration and Initial Actions {#incident-declaration-and-initial-actions}

Once a situation rises to the level of a formal incident, the first hour of response is the most consequential. Pynthia's program drives the Incident Commander and team through a structured first-hour checklist that addresses human safety verification, service stabilization, scope assessment, role assignment, initial notification, and establishment of a communication cadence — in that order and without shortcuts. Completing these steps in sequence ensures that the organization moves quickly from detection to coordinated response rather than allowing different parts of the team to pursue ad hoc actions simultaneously.

A formal Situation Report is produced within thirty minutes of declaration, capturing the current understanding of the incident's scope, impact, and status for distribution to leadership and key stakeholders. After that initial report, the program maintains a regular cadence of updated Situation Reports throughout the incident — issued every thirty to sixty minutes until the incident is stabilized or contained — so that decision-makers have current information and the response record is complete. The Incident Commander retains authority over declaration and Situation Report content, with a clear escalation path for incidents that develop into multi-region or multi-system events.

## Data Backup and Restore {#data-backup-and-restore}

The ability to recover member data and operational records is fundamental to the credit union's continuity posture. Pynthia maintains a tiered backup architecture designed around the recovery objectives set in the business impact analysis, with the most critical systems — including core banking, payment processing, and member account data — backed up on a continuous basis to immutable, offsite storage. The recovery point objective for critical tier systems is fifteen minutes, meaning that in the worst-case scenario the credit union should never lose more than fifteen minutes of critical transaction history. Each backup tier is tested quarterly through actual restore tests using a dedicated test environment, verifying not just that backups complete successfully but that the restoration process works correctly and within the required timeframe.

For ransomware and cryptographic-lock events specifically, the program uses clean point-in-time restores selected from a point prior to the compromise, rather than recovering from the most recent backup which may itself contain malicious material. Failed backup jobs generate immediate remediation tasks rather than being silently logged, and missed quarterly restore tests escalate to the Business Continuity Manager to prevent the testing program from falling behind without management awareness.

## Alternate Site and Remote Operations {#alternate-site-and-remote-operations}

When a physical facility becomes unavailable — whether due to a natural disaster, extended power outage, or other facility-level event — the program provides for two recovery modes depending on the nature and duration of the disruption. A pre-approved remote-work posture, with standardized VPN access and multi-factor authentication requirements, can be activated immediately for roles that can perform their critical functions remotely. For operations requiring physical presence or specialized infrastructure, the program maintains documented hot-site and virtual-site options with minimum staffing requirements.

The program targets alternate-site or remote readiness within eight hours of a facility-loss declaration, and full resumption of critical operations within twenty-four hours. Both targets reflect the recovery objectives derived from the business impact analysis for the services that cannot tolerate extended unavailability. Site capacity is tested on a recurring schedule so that the credit union maintains confidence that its alternate-site options can actually absorb the required workload when activated. For the Portugal office, alternate-site activations require additional coordination with local legal counsel to ensure that the arrangement for routing member data and directing employee activities complies with applicable European data-protection and employment law before the alternate configuration goes live.

## Major IT Failure Response {#major-it-failure-response}

Core-system and cloud-platform failures represent the most common category of significant operational disruption for a digital-first financial institution, and the program maintains a dedicated runbook for managing them through four phases: detection and initial triage, blast-radius isolation to prevent a single failure from cascading, rollback or failover to restore service, and member communication. The same five-minute Incident Commander assignment and fifteen-minute member-status standards that apply to all severe events apply here as well, reflecting the member-visibility consequences of payment-service or account-access outages.

Failover and rollback decisions — the choices about whether to cut over to a secondary system, revert to a prior software version, or invoke another form of service recovery — require dual-control approval to prevent unauthorized or inadvertent changes to production systems. This dual-control requirement is particularly important in high-pressure incident situations where the temptation to move quickly can override normal change-management discipline. Detailed technical runbooks and security-control specifications for cyber-originated IT failures live in the Information Security Policy; this control addresses the continuity-response lifecycle that wraps around those technical details.

## Incident Response — Privacy and Security {#incident-response-privacy-and-security}

Security and privacy incidents require a more structured response than operational outages because they carry distinct regulatory notification obligations, legal privilege considerations, and evidence-preservation requirements that must be respected from the first moment of response. On confirmation of a security incident, the program drives active containment to begin within one hour — stopping the spread of a compromise before it can affect additional systems or data. Within twenty-four hours, the Incident Commander and Business Continuity Manager engage legal counsel to assess reportability, privilege the investigation where appropriate, and begin the clock on regulatory notification obligations.

NCUA's rules on reportable cyber incidents impose a notification requirement with a specific deadline that the program tracks from the moment reasonable belief of a reportable event is established. Member notification obligations under applicable breach laws — which for a California-chartered institution include California state law in addition to federal requirements — have their own deadlines and content requirements that Compliance and Legal manage in coordination. For incidents involving the Portugal office or data routed through European systems, the program provides for parallel assessment of applicable European data-protection breach notification requirements. Where facts suggest criminal activity, the response includes a referral pathway to the BSA Officer. Detailed member breach-notice mechanics and the privacy-law analysis live in the Privacy Policy; this control addresses the response lifecycle and organizational coordination that surrounds those details.

## Communications and Notification Tree {#communications-and-notification-tree}

The ability to communicate effectively during a disruption is not guaranteed — in fact, many incidents impair the very communication tools the organization relies on. Pynthia's communications program addresses this by maintaining contact trees that span all stakeholder groups (employees across all four locations, Board members, regulators, critical vendors, and approved media spokespersons), pairing each primary communication channel with documented backup channels, and establishing predefined status-page playbooks for member-facing communication.

The first internal alert is issued within fifteen minutes of any activation, giving leadership and key personnel the initial situational awareness they need to begin their own preparedness steps. Regulator notifications are issued as required by law, with the specific deadlines applicable to each type of regulatory notice tracked through the incident record rather than left to individual judgment under pressure. Regulator contact information is verified on a recurring schedule so that the credit union is never in the position of having an outdated notification address at the moment a regulatory deadline is running. For incidents involving the Portugal office, internal communications with employees are coordinated with local HR and Legal given the labor-law constraints on how employer communications to employees are conducted under European and Portuguese employment regulation.

## People Continuity and Pandemic {#people-continuity-and-pandemic}

People are the most difficult resource to substitute in a continuity scenario, and the program addresses people-availability risk through three mechanisms: cross-training of personnel in essential roles so that no critical function depends on a single individual, split-team arrangements that reduce the probability of a single event eliminating the entire team for a given function, and pre-planned remote-work activation for eligible roles. These arrangements are maintained as live operational capabilities rather than theoretical contingencies, with staffing readiness reviewed on a recurring basis and coverage maps kept current.

The staffing activation threshold is set at thirty percent absenteeism across the enterprise or within a critical function — a level that indicates a systemic availability problem rather than routine individual absences — or upon a trigger from a public-health authority. At either trigger, the program activates the applicable staffing plan within twenty-four hours. For the Portugal office, the program maintains a Portugal-specific staffing annex developed in consultation with local legal counsel, because Portuguese labor law and EU employment regulation impose procedural requirements — including consultation obligations — that govern how emergency staffing measures can be implemented. These requirements cannot be overridden by the credit union's internal continuity program and must be reflected in the activation design.

## Post-Incident Review {#post-incident-review}

Every declared incident concludes with a formal Post-Incident Review that treats the event as a learning opportunity for the program as a whole. The review covers root cause analysis, an honest assessment of what worked and what failed in the response, and a corrective action plan that assigns specific remediation items to named owners with documented due dates. The PIR draft is completed within ten business days of containment, when the details are still fresh and the participants are still engaged with the response; the corrective action plan is approved within thirty days. Corrective items are tracked through to retest verification and formal closure rather than simply marked complete when the remediation work is done, providing assurance that the fix actually resolved the identified gap.

Where PIR findings implicate how the incident was characterized for regulatory purposes or whether notifications were handled correctly, those findings are escalated to Compliance and Legal before the PIR is closed, ensuring that the credit union has the opportunity to address any regulatory implications proactively. The cumulative body of PIRs and corrective action records is one of the most important demonstration artifacts for examiners, showing that the credit union's continuity program improves over time in response to real experience rather than remaining static.

## Vendor Contingency Management {#vendor-contingency-management}

Pynthia's critical operations depend on a network of technology and service vendors, many of whom also support the continuity capabilities the credit union relies on. A vendor outage, breach, or insolvency can create a continuity event regardless of how well-prepared the credit union's own infrastructure is, making vendor contingency management an essential component of the overall program. The program maintains current BCP and incident-response attestations, service-level agreements, recovery time and recovery point commitments, and disaster-recovery test evidence for all critical vendors, with evidence refreshed at least annually.

Exit and failover criteria are documented for each critical vendor relationship — specifying the conditions under which the credit union would invoke an exit or failover to an alternative provider — so that the decision can be made quickly during an incident based on pre-approved criteria rather than constructed under pressure. The program also assesses concentration risk and shared failure modes across the vendor portfolio, since multiple vendors relying on the same cloud provider or network infrastructure can fail simultaneously in ways that individual vendor assessments do not reveal. Vendor oversight for onboarding and ongoing due diligence lives in the Third-Party Risk Policy; this control addresses the continuity-specific attestation, testing, and contingency-planning elements of the vendor relationship.

## Governance and Accountability {#governance-and-accountability}

The Business Continuity Plan Policy is owned by Patrick Wilson, Chief Compliance Officer, who is accountable for program maintenance, control operation, and Board reporting. Patrick Wilson also serves as the approver. The program requires active participation from the Business Continuity Manager, the Incident Management Team, IT and site-reliability engineering, Human Resources, Vendor Risk management, and the Board of Directors. The Board's role is not passive; it approves the policy annually, receives exercise reports, and retains oversight of the program's effectiveness even as management carries out day-to-day execution.

The review cadence is designed to keep the program current with the credit union's evolving risk profile: the Board approves the policy at least annually; the IMT roster is verified quarterly; the business impact analysis is updated annually and certified quarterly for material changes; the threat register is refreshed annually and on interim material changes; and at least one enterprise exercise is completed each year with Board reporting within thirty days. Cross-references to related policies — the Information Security Policy, Third-Party Risk Policy, Privacy Policy, Record Retention Policy, Enterprise Risk Management Policy, and Electronic Payment Systems Policy — are maintained to ensure that the BCP program does not duplicate or conflict with those domains but instead integrates with them coherently.

## Open Items {#open-items}

Several elements of the program depend on engineering and operational confirmations that are in progress or subject to ongoing change and should be validated before the policy is treated as fully operational.

The vocabulary of system events, fields, timers, and records used in the companion structured policy reflects the target naming scheme of the credit union's core API; names are composed following the registered grammar and will be confirmed by engineering during the next review cycle. Any changes to naming that emerge from that confirmation will be reflected in a structured-policy revision but do not affect the program obligations described in this narrative.

The recovery time and recovery point objectives stated in this policy — including the fifteen-minute recovery point objective for critical systems, the eight-hour and twenty-four-hour alternate-site and operations targets, the five-minute Incident Commander assignment target, and the one-hour containment target — are taken from program design inputs and require validation against the credit union's contractual service-level agreements and the detailed recovery capabilities of its infrastructure before they can be relied upon as contractual commitments. That validation work is anticipated during the first operational cycle of the program.

The Portugal cross-border data-transfer and employment-law assessments referenced in the risk assessment, alternate-site, and people-continuity controls depend on legal analysis that must be kept current as European law and Portuguese employment regulation evolve. The credit union maintains those assessments with local legal counsel, but they represent an ongoing operational dependency rather than a one-time determination.

Finally, the reportability and notification timing obligations described in the incident response control reflect the credit union's current understanding of applicable law, including both federal NCUA notification requirements and California state breach-notification law. Both are subject to regulatory change and to fact-specific legal analysis in any actual incident; the program provides for prompt legal counsel engagement at the outset of any security incident specifically to ensure that notification obligations are assessed by counsel rather than by operational staff working from a fixed checklist.
