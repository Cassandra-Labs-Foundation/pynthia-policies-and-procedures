2.	POLICY STATEMENT



General



It is the policy of Any Bank that its liquidity be properly maintained to represent the Bank’s ability to fund assets and meet its obligations as they become due.  In the same respect, the Bank also recognizes the importance of liquidity and funds management at a reasonable cost and with a minimum of loss to effectively manage its balance sheet and related earnings stream.  In this respect, the Bank must be capable of meeting all obligations to customers at any time, and the active management of the Bank’s liquidity position is critical.  As such, the Board of Directors and Senior Management have formally adopted this policy and subsequent procedures in an effort to govern the management and control of liquidity, interest rate risk, and to provide adequate earnings in all plausible future interest rate environments.



Liquidity is essential to the Bank to compensate for expected and unexpected balance sheet fluctuations and provide funds for growth.  Liquidity risk is the risk of not being able to obtain funds at a reasonable price within a reasonable time period to meet obligations as they become due.  Liquidity strategies can be asset based (i.e., maintaining pools of highly liquid and marketable securities to meet unexpected funding needs) and/or liability based or off-balance sheet (i.e., funding partly through securitization, brokered/Internet deposits, or borrowings).  As such, the Bank must have adequate sources of on and off-balance sheet funds and liability based strategies available that can be acquired in time of need given the uncertain nature of customer demands in addition to the Bank’s desire to take advantage of earnings enhancement opportunities.  Accordingly, in addition to the liquidity provided by balance sheet cash flows, liquidity must be supplemented with additional sources such as credit lines with the Federal Home Loan Bank (FHLB), government sponsored enterprises (GSE), and other correspondence banks.



In this respect, liquidity management is among the most important activities that the Bank conducts because liquidity is critical to the ongoing viability of the Bank.



In general, the Bank’s funds management practices involve estimating and satisfying liquidity needs in the most cost effective way possible and without unduly sacrificing income potential.  Effective analysis and management of liquidity requires a measure of the liquidity position of the Bank on an ongoing basis and to examine how funding requirements are likely to evolve under various scenarios, including adverse conditions.



The formality and sophistication of the Bank’s Asset, Liability and Liquidity Management Program is based on the Bank’s:



Size and sophistication;



Nature and complexity of activities;



Strength and stability of core deposit base;



Dependence on wholesale funding;



Reliance on assets sales/securitizations for funding;



Variability of cash flows; and



Financial condition.



As such, the Bank utilizes various management information systems, strong analysis of funding requirements under alternative scenarios, diversification of funding sources, and contingency planning as crucial elements of the program.



The Bank realizes that the adequacy of its liquidity position will vary at different times, as similar liquidity positions may be adequate or inadequate depending on anticipated or unexpected funding needs.  Likewise, a liquidity position adequate for a peer bank may be inadequate for the Bank.  As such, determining the Bank's liquidity adequacy requires an analysis of the current liquidity position, present and anticipated asset quality, present and future earnings capacity, historical funding requirements, anticipated future funding needs, and options for reducing funding needs or obtaining additional funds by management.



One or a combination of the following events must occur for the Bank to adequately provide funds to satisfy its liquidity needs:



Disposal of assets.



Increase in short term borrowings and/or issuance of additional short term deposit and deposit like liabilities.



Increase in long term liabilities.



Increase in capital through earnings, capital injection, stock issuance, or issuance of other capital instruments.



Liquidity has a cost, which is a function of market conditions and the risk profile of the Bank.  If liquidity needs are met through holdings of high quality short term assets, generally the cost is the income sacrificed by not holding longer term and/or lower quality assets.  If funding needs are not met through liquid asset holdings, the Bank may be required to incur additional liabilities, possibly under adverse market conditions at an undesirable cost.



Program Overview



The Bank’s Asset, Liability and Liquidity Management Program consists of this and other Board of Directors approved policies and procedures that govern the Bank’s day to day management of liquidity and are communicated throughout the Bank.  It is the responsibility of Senior Management to inform the Board of Directors on a regular basis of the Bank’s liquidity situation, and it is the responsibility of the Board of Directors to ensure Senior Management properly monitors and controls the Bank’s liquidity risk.  This and other policies and procedures set and provide for the regular review of limits on the size of liquidity positions over particular time horizons.



The Bank’s management information systems provide an adequate method to measure, monitor, control and report liquidity risk, including regular reports provided to and reviewed by the Board of Directors, ALCO and Senior Management.  In general, these systems provide management with:



Timely information on the Bank’s current and prospective liquidity position;



Projections of the Bank’s liquidity position and liquidity requirements over various time horizons and scenarios;



Assumptions used in projections to evaluate the appropriateness and validity of the projections;



Data needed to determine compliance with this and the Bank’s other related policies, procedures, processes and limits;



Liquidity needs and sources to meet them, over various time horizons and scenarios based upon probability of occurrence;



List of funds providers;



Asset yields, liability costs, net interest margin, and variance from prior reporting periods or deviation from budget;



Long term interest margin trends;



Exceptions to policy guidelines;



Economic conditions in the Bank’s trade area, including interest rate projections and anticipated deviations from the original plan or budget; and



Information concerning nonrelationship or higher cost funding programs and their uses and costs.



In a normal business environment, Senior Management receives liquidity risk reports at least monthly, while the Board of Directors receives liquidity risk reports at least quarterly.  However, the Bank’s management information systems have the ability for reports to be produced with an increased frequency and/or on short notice if the need arises.  These reports are designed to provide Senior Management and the Board of Directors with a clear understanding of the Bank’s liquidity risk exposure, compliance with risk limits, consistency between management’s strategies and tactics, and consistency between these strategies and the Board of Director's expressed risk tolerance.



It is the responsibility of the Board of Directors and Senior Management to analyze liquidity under various scenarios, and the underlying assumptions for such scenarios is to be reviewed on a periodic basis as part of the process for the ongoing measurement of funding requirements.  In general, the following analytical techniques are used:



Financial Ratio Analysis.  The analysis of the Bank’s financial performance using selected financial ratios.



Intraday Liquidity Position Analysis.  Analysis of funds that the Bank can access during the business day with regards to significant payment, settlement, and clearing activities.  The Bank actively manages its intraday liquidity positions and risks to meet payment and settlement obligations on a timely basis under normal and stressed conditions, and has developed and adopted an intraday liquidity strategy that allows the Bank to: 



Monitor and measure expected daily gross liquidity inflows and outflows;



Manage and mobilize collateral when necessary to obtain intraday credit;



Identify and prioritize time specific and other critical obligations in order to meet them when expected;



Settle other less critical obligations as soon as possible;



Control credit to customers when necessary; and



Ensure that liquidity planners understand the amounts of collateral and liquidity needed to perform payment system obligations when assessing the organization’s overall liquidity needs.



Collateral Position Analysis.  The awareness of the different types of collateral and managing the effects of various covenants related to triggers, pricing, documentation, and netting agreements.  The Bank has the ability to calculate all of its collateral positions in a timely manner, including the value of assets currently pledged relative to the amount of security required and unencumbered assets available to be pledged.  This analysis monitors legal entity, jurisdiction, and currency exposure, and the Bank’s systems are capable of monitoring shifts between intraday and overnight or term collateral usage.  Management is made aware of the operational and timing requirements associated with accessing the collateral given its physical location (i.e., the custodian institution or securities settlement system with which the collateral is held).  In addition, management is made aware of the potential demand on required and available collateral arising from various types of contractual contingencies during periods of both market wide and Bank specific stress.



Maturity Gap and Funding Mismatch Analysis.  A liquidity gap schedule provides an analytical framework for measuring future funding needs by comparing the amount of assets and liabilities maturing over specific time intervals.



Cash Flow/Forecasting Analysis.  This analysis includes off-balance sheet items, such as draw downs on committed credit lines or collateral agreements in ISDA (international swaps and derivatives association) derivative contracts.



Liquidity Stress Testing and Scenario Analysis.  Stress testing analyzes the effect of changes in the association’s liquidity position.  Scenario analysis considers possible future events by analyzing alternative possible scenarios.  The analysis allows for improved decision making by consideration of different outcomes.



In accordance with the Bank’s VENDOR MANAGEMENT PROGRAM POLICY, relationships with lenders, other liability holders, and market participants are maintained in a diversified manner and reviewed on a periodic basis to ensure the Bank maintains a capacity to access funding through new borrowings or the sale of assets.  In the same respect, the Bank maintains contingency funding plans and scenarios include strategies for handling liquidity crisis and procedures for addressing cash flow shortfalls in emergency situations.



The Bank maintains an adequate system of internal controls that involves regular independent reviews and evaluations of the effectiveness of its liquidity management system to ensure that necessary and appropriate remedial steps are taken in accordance with the Bank’s AUDIT POLICY.



The Bank realizes that liquidity management includes evaluating various funding sources and the costs associated with the sources identified.  However, effective liquidity management does not necessarily mean that the Bank should employ the cheapest funding source available.  As such, management might opt to use a source that is not the cheapest in order to avoid a funding concentration, as concentrations in funding sources increase liquidity risk against the Bank.  This funding diversification allows the Bank to maintain access to different funding lines and allows more flexibility in selecting the appropriate funding source.  The frequency of contact with the lender and use of a funding source are two possible indicators of the strength of a funding relationship.  Also, in times of financial distress, the Bank will benefit from a diversified funding base rather than a situation where funding is concentrated in one source.  Diversification not only focuses on the number and type of potential funds providers and depositors, but also on the underlying stability, availability, and flexibility of sources.  In general, the Bank ensures diversification by analyzing:



Type and Product.  The reliance upon one type of funding or product is to be avoided, such as customer deposits, interbank borrowings, secured versus unsecured market funding, securitization vehicle, etc.



Counterparty.  No one counterparty is to represent more than a small fraction of the total funding of any type, whether depositors, interbank borrowings or securities borrowings.



Maturity Structure.  Ensure that funding corresponds to the Bank’s needs in terms of maturity and amount of funding for each maturity band.



Geographic Area.  Seek to diversify the Bank’s funding sources to limit its dependence on a given market, financial system, or geographical region.



Along with the cost of funds and diversification issues, the Bank considers maturity and repricing balance sheet mismatches, anticipated funding needs, and economic and market forecasts in its liquidity planning.



The Bank diversifies its available funding sources in the short, medium, and long term.  Diversification targets are part of the medium to long term funding plans and are aligned with the budgeting and business planning process.  Funding plans take into account correlations between sources of funds and market conditions.  Funding is also diversified across a full range of retail as well as secured and unsecured wholesale sources of funds, which are consistent with the Bank’s sophistication and complexity.  In addition, the Bank considers the funding implications of any government programs or guarantees it uses.  As with wholesale funding, the potential unavailability of government programs over the intermediate and long term is fully considered in the development of liquidity risk management strategies, tactics, and risk tolerances.  



An essential component of ensuring funding diversity is maintaining market access.  Market access is critical for effective liquidity risk management as it affects both the ability to raise new funds and to liquidate assets.  It is the responsibility of Senior Management and ALCO to ensure that market access is being actively managed, monitored, and tested by the appropriate staff.  Such efforts are to be consistent with the Bank’s liquidity risk profile and sources of funding.



The Bank has identified alternative sources of funding that strengthen its capacity to withstand a variety of severe institution specific and marketwide liquidity shocks.  Depending upon the nature, severity, and duration of the liquidity shock, potential sources of funding include, but are not limited to, the following: 



Deposit growth;



Lengthening maturities of liabilities;



Issuance of debt instruments;



Sale of subsidiaries or lines of business;



Asset securitization;



Sale (either outright or through repurchase agreements) or pledging of liquid assets;



Drawing down committed facilities; or



Borrowing.

3.	DEFINITIONS



Liquidity.  In general, liquidity is:



The ability to fund assets and meet obligations as they come due;



The amount the Bank holds in cash and other assets that are quickly convertible into cash without significant loss;



The Bank’s capacity to meet its financial obligations and commitments at reasonable or acceptable costs.



The essence of having cash when the Bank needs it.



Maintaining an adequate level of liquidity depends on the Bank’s ability to efficiently meet both expected and unexpected cash flows and collateral needs without adversely affecting either daily operations or the financial condition of the Bank.



Liquid Assets.  Liquid assets include any excess cash the Bank may have either in its vaults or on deposit with a Federal Reserve Bank or a correspondent Bank.  It also includes any non-loan earning asset that is unpledged and less than TIME PERIOD from maturity.  Types of liquid assets are listed below:



Cash on hand (including items in the process of collection);



Demand deposits due from banks;



U.S. treasury bills and notes;



Obligations due from federal agencies;



Certificates of deposit with other banks and savings and loan institutions due within TIME PERIOD;



Federal funds sold;



Money market instruments;



Municipal bonds due within TIME PERIOD.



Liquidity Risk.  The risk that the Bank’s financial condition or overall safety and soundness is adversely affected by an inability (or perceived inability) to meet its obligations.  The Bank’s obligations, and the funding sources used to meet them, depend significantly on its business mix, balance sheet structure, and the cash flow profiles of its on and off-balance sheet obligations.  In managing its cash flows, the Bank may confront various situations that can give rise to increased liquidity risk.  These include funding mismatches, market constraints on the ability to convert assets into cash or in accessing sources of funds (i.e., market liquidity), and contingent liquidity events.  Changes in economic conditions or exposure to credit, market, operation, legal, and reputation risks also can affect the Bank’s liquidity risk profile and should be considered in the assessment of liquidity and asset/liability management.



Non-Liquid Assets.  All other assets are deemed to be non-liquid.



Volatile Liabilities.  Volatile liabilities are deposits the Bank cannot depend on remaining with the Bank and include any liabilities the Bank may be forced to pay within the next six months.



Reliable Liabilities.  All other liabilities are deemed to be reliable.



4.	RISK MANAGEMENT



General



The Bank has identified the following primary risks that may affect its liquidity:



Reputation Risk.  Reputation risk is the current and prospective impact on earnings and capital arising from negative public opinion.  The Bank's reputation for meeting its obligations and operating in a safe and sound manner is essential to attracting funds at a reasonable cost and retaining funds during troubled times.  Negative public opinion, whatever the cause, may prompt depositors, other funds providers, and investors to seek greater compensation, such as higher rates or additional credit support, for maintaining deposit balances with the Bank or conducting any other business with it.  If negative public opinion continues, withdrawals of funding could become debilitating.  To minimize reputation risk and its potential impact on liquidity, the Bank:



Continually assesses its reliance on credit sensitive funding;



Diversifies the sources and tenors of market funding; and



Increases asset liquidity, as appropriate.



Strategic Risk.  Strategic risk is the current and prospective impact on earnings or capital arising from adverse business decisions, improper implementation of decisions, or lack of responsiveness to industry changes.  As such, the Bank periodically considers the impact on its funding abilities when creating strategic goals, objectives or during times of rapid growth to avoid liquidity risk to reach an unacceptable level.  In this respect, the Bank must be able to raise money required to meet its obligations at an affordable cost.



Credit Risk.  Credit risk is the current and prospective risk to earnings or capital arising from an obligor’s failure to meet the terms of any contract with the Bank or otherwise to perform as agreed.  In the event the Bank decides to assume more credit risk through asset concentrations or adoption of new underwriting standards in conjunction with untested business lines, it will properly evaluate the impact this action will have on its liquidity risk.



Interest Rate Risk.  Interest rate risk is the current and prospective risk to earnings or capital arising from movements in interest rates.  Changes in interest rates affect income earned from assets and the cost of funding those assets.  A change in interest rates also affects the economic value of the Bank’s balance sheet.  For example, the present value of most investment securities decreases in a rising rate environment.  To maintain the total value of assets serving as collateral in repurchase agreements or pledged against deposits, the Bank may have to pledge or encumber additional securities, increasing its cost of funds.  The cost of alternative funding sources may also increase as depositors and other lenders demand market interest rates in a rising rate environment.  Off-balance sheet instruments that the Bank uses to manage its interest rate risk may also pose liquidity risk.  The cash flows of those instruments are often very sensitive to changes in rates, and if not properly managed, can result in unexpected funding requirements or other cash outflows during periods of volatile interest rates.



Refer to the Bank’s INTEREST RATE RISK POLICY for detailed guidance.



Price Risk.  Price risk (or market risk) is the risk to earnings or capital arising from changes in the value of traded portfolios of financial instruments.  Price risk may result in volatile earnings.  Price risk is closely monitored by funds providers when assessing the Bank's financial position and creditworthiness.  If price risk and its perceived impact on earnings or capital is too great, funds providers may require the Bank to pay increased rates for funds, may not be willing to invest in longer term maturities, or may not be willing to provide funding on any terms.



Transaction Risk.  Transaction risk is the current and prospective risk to earnings and capital arising from fraud, error, and the inability to deliver products or services, maintain a competitive position and manage information.  Systems that directly affect liquidity include wire transfer systems for check and securities clearing, electronic banking, and operations governing credit, debit, and smart card usage.  If product lines change, the Bank must adjust the systems to ensure that all transactions can be handled.  Significant problems can develop very quickly if the systems that process transactions fail or delay execution.  If customers have difficulty accessing their accounts, they may close them, which will diminish liquidity.  As such, transaction risk is considered in the Bank's contingency planning process.



Liability Based or Off-balance Sheet Strategy Risk



With respect to the use of liability based or off-balance sheet liquidity strategies, the Bank realizes that traditional measures of liquidity, such as the ratio of loans to deposits or non-core funding dependency, may not provide an accurate view of the Bank's true liquidity position.  In addition, an increase in use of liability based and off-balance sheet strategies will elevate the Bank’s liquidity risk profile.  As such, it is the policy of the Bank to take a forward looking approach to this liquidity planning strategy.  The Bank augments traditional liquidity risk measures with pro forma cash flow and scenario analysis to ensure that it maintains realistic contingency funding plans that are responsive to changes in liquidity risk exposure.  In this respect, the Bank utilizes liquidity measurement tools that match its funds management strategies that provide a comprehensive view of the Bank's liquidity risk.  Risk limits are approved by the Board of Directors and are to be consistent with the measurement tools used.



The Bank realizes that obtaining or retaining funding sources during times of financial stress may be difficult.  In this respect, the terms associated with wholesale borrowings (both secured and unsecured) can become more restrictive when the Bank faces either real or perceived financial difficulties.  In the event the Bank becomes less than “well capitalized” in accordance with Section 38 of the Federal Deposit Insurance Act, it may face limits on the ability to accept, renew, or roll over brokered or high cost deposits in accordance with the Joint Agency Advisory on Brokered and Rate Sensitive Deposits.  In addition, if the Bank uses securitization it can face early amortization events resulting from market disturbances, deterioration in the Bank's financial condition, or the performance of securitized assets.  These risks are more pronounced if the Bank has concentrated business lines or funding sources.



The Bank utilizes pro forma cash flow statements as part of its liquidity analysis for liability based or off-balance sheet funding strategies that hold significant amounts of assets with cash flows that depend on choices borrowers can make to prepay or extend their obligations, or that have off-balance sheet funding commitments.  Pro forma cash flow analysis shows the Bank's projected sources and uses of funds under various liquidity scenarios, identifying potential funding shortfalls or gaps.  It is the responsibility of the Chief Financial Officer and Senior Management to limit these exposures and report them to the Board of Directors along with a plan to address significant potential funding shortfalls which is commensurate with the complexity of the Bank’s liquidity risk profile.  The pro forma cash flow analysis is to incorporate multiple scenarios that consider the general and unique risks faced by the Bank.  In addition, assumptions used in the pro forma cash flow projections are to be reasonable and appropriate, and are to consider a wide range of potential outcomes with regard to the stability of retail and larger deposits, brokered deposits, public funds, borrowings, Internet deposits, and the retention rate of funds obtained through deposit specials.  In the event the Bank relies on securitization, sensitivity tests are to be performed to measure the effects that material changes to assumptions would have on related accounts.



Risk Mitigation



It is the responsibility of ALCO and Senior Management to monitor various internal and external market indicators of potential liquidity problems at the Bank.  These indicators, while not necessarily requiring drastic corrective action, may prompt ALCO, Senior Management and the Board of Directors to conduct additional monitoring or analysis procedures.  Examples of such internal indicators are:



A negative trend or significantly increased risk in any area or product line, such as loan delinquencies, cancellation of loan commitments, and/or not renewing maturing loans.



Concentrations in either assets or liabilities;



A decline in indicators of asset quality;



A decline in earnings performance or projections;



Rapid asset growth funded by volatile wholesale liabilities or brokered deposits;



Real or perceived negative publicity, such as negative third party opinions and evaluations from professional market analysts or other participants regarding concerns about the Bank’s credit capacity, including but not limited to:



The description of a “troubled bank” in market rumors;



Downgrades or announcements of potential downgrades of the Bank’s credit rating by agencies; or



Customers contacting relationship managers, fixed income sales representatives, and branch employees requesting information.



Bearish secondary market activity in the Bank's securities may signal declining value, such as:



Drop in stock price;



Wider secondary spreads on the Bank's senior and subordinated debt, and increasing trading of the Bank's debt; or



Brokers/dealers are reluctant to show the Bank's name in the market, forcing Senior Management to arrange "friendly" broker/dealer support.



The Bank's funding market may begin to contract or demand credit support, better credit terms, or shorter duration lending, any of which may increase liquidity costs, such as:



Overall funding costs increase;



Counterparties increase collateral requirements or demand collateral for accepting credit exposure to the Bank;



Correspondent banks eliminate or decrease credit line availability, causing the Bank to make larger purchases in the brokered funds market;



Volume of turndowns in the brokered markets is unusually large, forcing the Bank to deal directly with fewer willing counterparties;



Rating sensitive providers, such as trust managers, money managers, and public entities, abandon the Bank;



Counterparties and brokers are unwilling to deal in unsecured or longer term transactions;



Transaction sizes are decreasing, and some counterparties are unwilling to enter into even short dated transactions;



The Bank receives requests from depositors for early withdrawal of their funds, or the Bank has to repurchase its paper in the market; or



An increasing spread paid on deposits relative to local competitors, or national or regional composites.



Significant acquisitions by the Bank;



Decreasing weighted average maturity of liabilities;



Violation of self imposed liquidity limits;



Over reliance on or significant increase in the level of wholesale funding;



Excessive borrowing concentrations;



Significant concentrations within the asset portfolio or of funding sources;



Increasing deposit withdrawals;



A sharp rise in funding costs;



A sharp decline in earnings and interest margins;



Increase in spread paid on deposits, borrowed funds, or asset securitizations between the Bank and its peers;



Short interest or position (when a person sells stocks they do not yet own) in the Bank (or its holding company), thus leading investors to believe the Bank’s stock price will drop;



Pressure on the Bank to buy back loans or securities the Bank sold in the secondary market;



A strategy of granting higher risk loan products such as subprime loans and nontraditional loans, (option ARM loans, interest only loans, etc.) with a heavy reliance on selling into the secondary markets, particularly when the Bank funds these originations from wholesale funding sources;



An increase in nonperforming assets;



A decline in capital adequacy category;



Management problems; or



A sharp increase in putbacks of loans previously sold.



Liquidity Risk Limit Guidelines



The Bank has established liquidity risk tolerances and/or limits that are appropriate for its business strategy, taking into consideration the Bank’s complexity, business mix, liquidity risk profile, and its role in the financial system, including quantitative targets and qualitative guidelines.  These limits, tolerances, and guidelines may include items such as:



Discrete or cumulative cash flow mismatches or gaps (sources and uses of funds) over specified future short and long term time horizons under both expected and adverse business conditions.  Often, these are expressed as cash flow coverage ratios or as specific aggregate amounts.



Target amounts of unencumbered liquid asset reserves expressed as aggregate amounts or as ratios.



Measures used to identify unstable liabilities and liquid asset coverage ratios.  For example, these may include ratios of wholesale funding to total liabilities, potentially volatile retail (e.g., high cost or out of market) deposits to total deposits, and other liability dependency measures, such as short term borrowings as a percent of total funding. 



Asset concentrations that could increase liquidity risk through a limited ability to convert to cash, such as complex financial instruments (which are illiquid, difficult to value, or marked by the presence of cash flows that are irregular, uncertain or difficult to model), bank owned (corporate owned life insurance, and less marketable loan portfolios).



Funding concentrations that address diversification of funding sources and types, such as large liability and borrowed funds dependency, secured versus unsecured funding sources, exposures to single providers of funds, exposures to funds providers by market segments, and different types of brokered deposits or wholesale funding.



Funding concentrations that address the term, repricing, and market characteristics of funding sources with consideration given to the nature of the assets they fund.  This may include diversification targets for short, medium, and long term funding, instrument type and securitization vehicles, and guidance on concentrations for currencies and geographical markets.



Contingent liability exposures such as unfunded loan commitments, lines of credit supporting asset sales or securitizations, and collateral requirements for derivatives transactions and various types of secured lending.



Exposures of material activities, such as securitization, derivatives, trading, transaction processing, and international activities, to broad systemic and adverse financial market events.

Liquidity Stress Test and Scenario Analysis.  Liquidity stress and scenario analysis is a critical aspect of liquidity risk management.  It is the responsibility of ALCO and Senior Management to conduct stress testing and scenario analysis in estimating the Bank’s liquidity requirements.  The magnitude and frequency of stress testing is to be commensurate with the complexity of the Bank and the level of its risk exposures.  It is especially important after long periods of good economic and financial conditions.  In addition, it is very important during periods of expansion, when new products or rapid growth occurs for which no loss data is available.  Liquidity stress and scenario analysis is to be performed on a periodic basis to:



Assess exposures;



Identify and quantify sources of potential liquidity strain and weaknesses;



Analyze possible impacts on the Bank’s cash flows, liquidity position, profitability and solvency;



Ensure the validity of liquidity planning relative to occurrences of internal turbulence at the Bank and/or to occurrences of stress, crisis, and collapse in the financial markets; and



Ensure current exposures are consistent with the Bank’s established liquidity risk tolerance.



This analysis is to be robust and relevant to the Bank’s current business model, balance sheet composition, and overall operations.  The analysis is to be capable of addressing a wide range of “what if” scenarios.



Liquidity stress testing and scenario analysis involves assessing liquidity under a variety of stresses and scenarios considering:



Short, intermediate, and long term scenarios;



Bank specific problems and issues; and



Systemic liquidity problems.



Stress testing and scenario analysis is to combine several related events and not be limited to a series of isolated instances as model based analysis tools often fail to account for event correlation, such as the following possibilities:



Price shocks for specific asset categories;



Evaporation of asset liquidity, significant losses by the Bank; and



Growth of liquidity needs because of commitments, economic conditions, recourse arrangements, off-balance sheet risks and a lack of access to funding markets.



In conducting an analysis of liquidity, ALCO and Senior Management are to consider an extreme range of possible future scenarios, such as optimistic, pessimistic, and most likely.



In estimating normal funding needs, the Bank may use historical data and account for seasonal and other effects believed to determine loan demand and deposit flows.  Alternatively, the Bank may rely on judgmental business projections, or undertake a customer by customer assessment for larger customers and apply historical relationships to the remainder.  In any respect, the following factors are to be considered:



Stressful events such as a loss of wholesale funding, a significant runoff of deposits, a sharp increase in funding costs, a sharp increase in loan demand, or a loss of access to securitization markets;



No additional borrowing capacity through the FHLB and/or the Federal Reserve Bank, in addition to collateral requirement increases on borrowing lines;



Deposit runoff due to adverse financial events or media, with special emphasis on concentrations in noninsured, commercial, high rate, and other potentially sensitive deposits;



A downgrade in the Bank’s PCA category from less than Well Capitalized (or equivalent triggering events), and the associated impacts on liquidity including brokered and high rate deposits;



Cash flow timing differences and the related assumptions among scenarios, such as in a general market crisis, the ability to sell assets may deteriorate significantly; and



The potential for unanticipated cash outflows and reduced cash inflows associated with embedded options in various assets, liabilities, and off-balance-sheet contacts.  Potential cash outflows include loan commitments, calls on loans sold with recourse and financial guarantees, payments on swap contracts and other financial derivatives, margin calls, early termination agreements, etc.



It is the responsibility of ALCO and Senior Management to discuss the results of stress tests and take remedial or mitigating actions to limit the Bank’s exposures, build up a liquidity cushion, and adjust its liquidity profile to fit its risk tolerance.  This stress testing and scenario analysis process helps management in formulating the Bank’s liquidity contingency funding plan.  As such, stress testing and contingency planning are closely intertwined.

Spread Management



The objective of managing the rate sensitivity gap is to avoid negative spread.  Spread is managed by loan and deposit pricing, and investing.  The Bank strives to have neither the lowest nor the highest loan or deposit rates in the market.  Loans are priced in order to achieve a fair return on shareholder investment.  Deposits are priced to provide fair treatment for our customers and to be reasonably competitive without increasing the Bank's cost of funds.



The Bank's spread management goal is to price loans and deposits in order to obtain an interest spread of PERCENT.  That goal is based on the following components:



Yield on Earning Assets.  The yield on earning assets is the Bank's interest income (including loan fees) divided by its average earning assets;



Cost to Fund Earning Assets.  The cost to fund earning assets is the Bank's interest expense divided by its earning assets; and



Interest Spread.  The interest spread is the yield on earning assets less the cost to fund earning assets.



Refer to the Bank’s INTEREST RATE RISK POLICY for detailed guidance.



Net Liquid Assets, Liquidity Ratio and Pricing



The Bank utilizes a liquidity chart spreadsheet to estimate the liquid and non-liquid portions of each asset category, in addition to the volatile and reliable portions of each liability account.  To complete the process, all Bank liquid assets and all volatile liabilities are totaled.  The volatile liabilities are subtracted from the liquid assets to arrive at the Bank's liquidity position.  Zero liquidity is defined as being neither liquid nor non-liquid.  Positive liquidity numbers represent excess liquidity and negative liquidity numbers represent a non-liquid position.



By dividing the liquidity position by total average assets, the Bank arrives at a liquidity ratio.  The Bank's assets and liabilities are managed to achieve a liquidity ratio of at least +RATIO.  Since the liquidity position can be positive or negative, the ratio can be positive or negative.  A positive liquidity position means only that the Bank is more liquid than it needs to be.  A positive liquidity position is not a danger signal.  However, it may mean that the Bank is sacrificing profits unnecessarily to achieve a liquidity position that is too liquid.  A negative liquidity position represents too much non-liquidity and is cause for concern.  If the liquidity ratio is negative and larger than RATIO, the Bank is approaching a position that is dangerously non-liquid.



The yield the Bank receives on loans and investments is important only when safety and soundness requirements have been met.  With the possible execution of certain “rate sensitive” consumer markets, it is the policy of the Bank to be a SERVICE INTENSIVE financial institution which prices its deposits fairly but does not “buy” deposit business.  Large demand relationships and other financial considerations can influence CD pricing, however the Bank anticipates that it should be able to adequately fund its loan portfolio and provide adequate liquidity without engaging in above market deposit pricing.  As such, appropriate discipline in this area will have a considerable effect on the Bank’s bottom line.



It is the responsibility of ALCO and the Board of Directors to carefully and continually monitor pricing of transaction and non-transaction deposit rates and provide fair and competitive pricing while attempting to minimize interest expense.



NOTE:  The Bank realizes that it may be necessary to use “over market” pricing to fund short term borrowings on a rare occasion.  This can occur when the Bank experiences a rapid growth in loans and core deposits have not kept pace.  When this occurs, the Bank will use the shortest term of CDs possible.  Such stop gap funding may be classified as “brokered deposits” under FDICIA.  A maximum of PERCENT of the Bank’s deposits may be obtained from such “over market rate” CDs.  Repurchase agreements can also be used for this purpose.  As such, it is the responsibility of ALCO and the Board of Directors to retard loan growth should such funding be needed on an ongoing basis.

Overview of Funding Assets



Liquidity needs may be met by managing the Bank's asset structure through the sale or planned pay down of assets.  As previously stated, assets ideally have a PERCENT or more loan to deposit mix that will be maintained.  At this level, the Bank can enjoy high earnings coupled with adequate liquidity so long as credit quality and overhead are controlled.



Fed funds and other instantly available investments should provide a minimum PERCENT coverage of the Bank’s deposit base.  Periodic volatility of deposit levels may require a high number.



Other surplus funds may be placed as appropriate in other approved investments as stated in the Bank’s INVESTMENT POLICY.



The Bank’s loan portfolio is designed to be diversified as to loan category, collateral coverage, and industry concentration.  In any respect, over concentration in one or more areas is avoided by the Bank.  Loans within the Bank’s portfolio can be used as collateral for secured borrowings or sold for cash in the secondary loan market.  Sales in the secondary market provide fee income, relief from interest rate risk, and a funding source to the originating bank.



Loan commitments, such as fee paid letters of credit used as backup lines, are traditional uses of funds that are off-balance sheet.  The Bank estimates the amount of unfunded commitments that will require funding over various time horizons.  This estimate is included with anticipated demands against unfunded commitments in the Bank’s internal reporting and contingency planning process.



In any respect, the Bank realizes that assets normally assumed to be liquid sometimes are not liquidated easily and/or profitably.  For example, investment securities may be pledged against public funds and repurchase agreements, or may be depreciated heavily because of interest rate changes.  On the other hand, holding liquid assets for liquidity purposes becomes less attractive because of thin profit margins and capital maintenance requirements.  As such, the Bank strives to maintain a proper level of liquid assets through the stability of its funding structure and the potential for rapid loan portfolio expansion.  Generally, if the sources of funds are stable, established yet unused borrowing capacity is significant, and loan demand is predictable, a relatively low allowance for liquidity is required.  Factors that may indicate that a higher allowance for liquidity is required include:



The competitive environment is such that Bank customers can invest in alternative instruments;



Recent trends show substantial reduction in large liability accounts;



Substantial deposits are short term municipal special assessment type accounts;



A substantial portion of the loan portfolio consists of large problem credits with little likelihood of reduction or marketability;



A substantial portion of the loan portfolio consists of non-marketable loans (e.g., longer term, non-amortizing, non-homogeneous loans that may not be readily marketable);



The Bank expects customers to draw upon unused lines of credit or commitments in the near future;



A concentration of credits has been extended to an industry with present or anticipated financial problems;



A close relationship exists between individual demand accounts and principal employers in the trade area who have financial problems;



A significant portion of assets is pledged to support wholesale borrowings; or



Access to the capital markets is impaired.



To balance profitability and liquidity, it is the responsibility of ALCO and Senior Management to carefully weigh the full return on liquid assets (yield plus insurance value) against the expected higher return associated with less liquid assets.  Income derived from higher yielding assets may be offset if a forced sale is necessary due to adverse balance sheet fluctuations.



With respect to asset securitization, the Bank essentially transforms a pool of assets into cash.  Asset securitization typically involves the transfer or sale of on balance sheet assets to a third party who issues asset backed securities that are sold to investors in the public debt market.  Investors in these securities are paid from the cash flow received from the transferred assets.  Assets that are typically securitized include credit card receivables, automobile receivables, commercial and residential mortgage loans, commercial loans, home equity loans, and student loans.



Securitization can be an effective funding method for the Bank.  However, the Bank realizes that there are several risks associated with using securitization as a funding source such as:



Some securitizations have early amortization clauses to protect investors if the performance of the underlying assets does not meet pre-specified criteria.  If an early amortization clause is triggered, the issuing institution must begin paying principal to bondholders earlier than originally anticipated and will have to fund new receivables that would have otherwise been transferred to the trust.  The issuing institution must monitor deal performance to anticipate cash flow and funding ramifications due to early amortization clauses.



If the issuing institution has a large concentration of residual assets, the institution's overall cash flow might be dependent on the residual cash flows from the performance of the underlying assets.  If the performance of the underlying assets is worse than projected, the institution's overall cash flow will be less than anticipated.



An issuer's marketplace reputation is crucial to its ability to generate cash from future securitizations.  If this reputation is damaged, issuers might not be able to economically securitize assets and generate cash from future sales of loans to the trust.  This is especially true for institutions that are relatively new to the securitization market.  Also, if loans held for sale are funded with short term funding, the institution will have to find alternative funding sources if it is not able to sell these assets quickly.



Residual assets that the issuing institution retains are typically non-liquid assets, for which there is no active market.  Additionally, these assets are not acceptable collateral to pledge for borrowings.



In the case of the securities portfolio, the Bank may discover that securities listed as available for sale may be needed to pledge additional collateral to secure public funds or other borrowing lines.  For example, a financial institution was initially required to pledge collateral for public funds at 25 percent of the average public funds balance.  After the examination results required significant Call Report amendments, the public entity increased the collateral requirement to 125 percent of the average balance.  With no additional collateral available, the financial institution was forced to use cash as collateral to retain the deposits.  As such, a plan to sell securities as a source of liquidity depends on its effectiveness on the credit quality and marketability of these securities.



The Bank realizes that with adequate planning and certain efficiencies, securitizations can create a more liquid balance sheet, as well as leverage origination capacity.  However, peculiarities related to certain transactions, in addition to excessive reliance on securitizations as a single funding vehicle, may increase liquidity risk.  For example, a concentration or over reliance on securitizations as a funding source may increase liquidity risk if there are disruptions in the market.  Therefore, it is the responsibility of ALCO and Senior Management to carefully consider how securitizations affect the Bank’s day to day liquidity management and its contingency funding plan.  ALCO and Senior Management are to analyze the potential effect of securitizations on liquidity from an individual transaction perspective and on an aggregate basis, and make the following determinations when contemplating a securitization transaction:



The type of security (the agency securitization market remains active, but the non-agency securitization market is not);



The volume of securities scheduled to amortize during any particular period;



The plans for meeting future funding requirements (including when such requirements may arise);



The existence of early amortization or increased collateralization triggers;



The alternatives available for obtaining substantial amounts of liquidity quickly; and



The operational concerns associated with reissuing securities.



In the event the Bank uses securitizations to fund credit cards and other revolving credit receivables, ALCO and Senior Management are to prepare for the possible return of receivable balances to the balance sheet because of scheduled or early amortization.  Return of receivable balances because of scheduled or early amortization may result in large asset pools that require balance sheet funding at unexpected or inopportune times.  In addition, ALCO and Senior Management are to factor the maturity and potential funding needs of the receivables into short, intermediate, and long term liquidity planning.



Exposure may also increase if the Bank minimizes securitization costs by structuring transactions at maturities offering the lowest cost, without regard to maturity concentrations or potential long term funding requirements.  Correlating maturities of incidental securitized transactions with overall planned balance sheet growth may somewhat mitigate this risk.



Traditionally, the Bank realizes that loans are not as marketable as securities, and distressed loans are even less marketable.  Bids may be severely discounted given a financial institution’s stressed condition, and a deteriorating capital position may prevent the financial institution from realizing the sale.  Due diligence for loan sales requires time and effort.  In the event the Bank considers this option, it is the responsibility of ALCO, Senior Management and the Board of Directors to establish such business relationships by completing initial due diligence requirements in accordance with the Bank’s VENDOR MANAGEMENT PROGRAM POLICY.  Asset sales or nonrecourse loan participations may negatively affect interest income, but they can also provide short term liquidity.

In addition to serving as a supplement to asset liquidity, liability sources may serve as an alternative even when asset sources are available.  In any respect, the evaluation of wholesale funding by the Bank is commensurate with the degree of risk faced and used in accordance with this and other funds management policies, risk limits, strategic plans, and management expertise.

For that reason it is the responsibility of Bank personnel responsible for managing overall liquidity to be aware of any information (such as an announcement of a decline in earnings or a downgrading by a rating agency) that could affect the market's or public's perception of the soundness of the Bank.

Bank Investment Contracts



A Bank Investment Contract (BIC) is a deposit contract between the Bank and its customer that permits the customer to deposit funds over a period of time and obligates the Bank to repay the amounts deposited plus interest at a guaranteed rate to the end of the contract term.  The contract term varies, and may range from six months to as long as ten years.  BICs are generally structured as non-transferable liabilities (i.e., not saleable in a secondary market).  Typically, customers for BICs are in most cases sponsors of employee benefit plans such as pension plans or deferred compensation plans.  BICs may provide specified periods and conditions under which additional deposits or withdrawals may be made to or from such contracts.  Therefore, the Bank's liquidity planning includes making a reasonable estimate of its cash flow from BIC funding under different interest rate scenarios.



BIC deposits are limited to PERCENT of the Bank’s total asset base.  Board of Directors approval is required to exceed these limits.



Borrowed Funds



Ensuring that dependable borrowing facilities are in place provides an additional source of liquidity to the Bank and represents an integral part of its liquidity and funds management practices.  This subtopic addresses the role and use of alternative funding sources such as FHLB advances and wholesale repurchase agreements.  Because the nature and extent of wholesale funding activities will depend greatly on the Bank’s overall balance sheet risk position, the administration of these activities primarily falls under the responsibility of ALCO.



The appropriate use of asset and liability funding sources may result in lower overall liquidity cost.  By managing borrowings in a coordinated fashion with asset liquidity needs, the Bank can tailor liabilities to fit its cash flow needs instead of apportioning asset types and amounts to a given liability base.  Locking in term funding can also reduce liquidity risk, especially if the Bank can extend the duration of its liability structure.  Accessing wholesale funds allows the Banks to obtain funds quickly and efficiently.  Nevertheless, borrowings are viewed by the Bank as a supplemental funding source, rather than as a replacement for core deposits.



Managing liquidity through adjustments to liabilities requires the Bank to plan strategies more carefully than if the Bank managed liquidity based only on assets.  As such, it is the responsibility of ALCO and Senior Management to have a complete understanding of the associated risks, commensurate risk management practices, and a comprehensive contingency funding plan that specifically addresses funding as the Bank's financial condition or the economy deteriorates when relying on borrowed funds.



The Bank considers the following key elements of risk with respect to borrowed funds:



Secured borrowings can impact the Bank's liquidity profile by pledging high quality assets, lessening the availability of such assets for contingent liquidity demands.



If the Bank's condition or the economic climate deteriorates, it will be more difficult to borrow funds economically, if at all, when needed the most.



Changes in market conditions can make it difficult for the Bank to secure funds and to manage its funding maturity structure.



Due to rate competition, the Bank may incur relatively high costs in obtaining funds and may lower credit quality standards in order to invest in higher yielding loans and securities.  If the Bank is purchasing liabilities to support assets already on its books, the high cost of borrowings may result in a negative yield spread.



Preoccupation with obtaining funds at the lowest possible cost, without proper consideration given to diversification and to maturity distribution, intensifies the Bank's exposure to funding concentrations and the risk of interest rate fluctuations, respectively.



Management might not fully understand the terms of the particular borrowings.  Some borrowings have embedded options that make maturity or future interest rate uncertain.  This uncertainty can increase the complexity of liquidity management and, under certain circumstances, may increase the cost of funding.



The extent of ALCO and Senior Management’s reaction to these risks will depend upon the mix of funding sources and the Bank’s risk tolerance (the willingness and ability of an individual or the Bank to borrow or lend money for a given risk/reward profile).  Factors affecting risk tolerance of funds providers include:



Obligations to fiduciary investors, such as money market funds, trust funds and pensions.



Reliance on rating firms.  Bylaws or internal guidelines may prohibit placing funds in banks that have low ratings.



Obligations to disclose information on investment holdings.



Self interest in maintaining an orderly marketplace.  For this reason major banks are slow in eliminating funding to other banks.



Lack of a personal contact at the bank to provide timely and accurate information about its financial condition. 



The limit established on all types of borrowings is set at PERCENT of total assets.  Board of Directors approval is required to exceed these limits.



Wholesale funding will typically be considered in the following situations:



To supplement outflows of the deposit base;



When there is a need to acquire funds quickly without impacting local market pricing;



When there is an inability to fund a specific transaction from the local market with the type/term of funds desired (such as with funds that fit the existing balance sheet structure); or



When the cost of such funds is below the marginal cost of raising local market deposits.



In addition, when deemed appropriate for purposes of managing interest rate risk and/or improving the utilization of Bank capital, wholesale funding may be used to purchase investment securities and finance activities.  However, the Board of Directors and Senior Management recognize that the terms and repricing characteristics of such borrowing and investment activities may be intentionally mismatched to achieve specific ALCO objectives.



Federal Home Loan Bank (FHLB) Advances.  The Bank is authorized to borrow when necessary from the FHLB.  FHLB advances tend to be the Bank’s primary borrowing vehicle due to both the dependability of the FHLB as a provider of funds and the availability of a broad range of maturities (overnight to 10 years or longer) with a variety of characteristics that can be tailored to the needs of the Bank.  While the FHLB lends on a collateralized basis with 1-4 family mortgages and U.S. Government and Agency securities being the collateral of choice, other loan types also qualify to varying degrees.  The Bank realizes that the FHLB may limit or deny the Bank’s request for an advance if the Bank is:



Engaging in any unsafe or unsound practice;



Inadequately capitalized;



Sustaining operating losses; and



Deficient with respect to financial or managerial resources or deficient otherwise.



Generally, the FHLB requires that collateral secure advances.  Thus, the unused borrowing capacity of the Bank is a function of both its eligible, unpledged collateral and its unused line of credit with the FHLB.  In addition, the Bank may need to increase its holdings of FHLB stock in order to access all unused borrowing capacity (FHLBs generally require members to hold stock equal to approximately five percent of outstanding advances).



Some FHLB advances contain embedded options or other features that may increase funding risk.  For example, some types of advances provide the FHLB with the option to demand repayment or increase the interest rate on the advance under specified conditions.

 

Sometimes before market information is available to other fund providers a FHLB can react quickly to reduce its exposure to a troubled institution by not rolling over unsecured lines of credit.  Depending on the severity of a troubled institution’s condition, a FHLB may take the following actions:



Apply steeper “haircuts” (the margin or difference between the actual market value of a security and the value assessed by the lending side of the transaction) to existing advances.



Apply more conservative valuations of underlying collateral.



Reduce the available line relative to total assets.



Discontinue or withdraw (at maturity) its collateralized funding program because of concerns about the quality or reliability of the collateral or other credit related concerns.



This may create significant liquidity problems for the Bank, especially if it has large amounts of short term FHLB funding.  As such, it is the policy of the Bank to aggregate FHLB funds by type of program to monitor and appropriately limit short term liability concentrations, just as with any other credit sensitive funds provider.



For FHLB borrowings (as with all borrowings to meet liquidity needs), it is the policy of the Bank to evaluate the level of its borrowings from any one source as well as the quality of the source.  It is the responsibility of ALCO and Senior Management to perform adequate due diligence in selecting funding sources, and periodically review their quality and stability.  Significant concentrations of funding from an FHLB are a risk factor and the Bank controls that risk and has viable contingency plans in place should a need arise for alternative funding sources.



Repurchase Agreements (Repo).  Securities sold under repurchase agreements are a means of financing inventories of securities.  Under repurchase agreements, securities are temporarily “loaned out,” for periods ranging from overnight to one year in return for borrowed funds.  The vast majority of repurchase agreements mature in three months or less.  A standard repurchase agreement involves the acquisition of funds through the sale of securities with a simultaneous commitment to repurchase the securities on a specified date at a specified price.  The collateral most often used by the Bank is U.S. government and agency mortgage backed securities (MBS).  The repurchase agreement rate is the interest rate that the borrower pays the lender (investor) for the use of funds.



When appropriate the Bank may utilize such borrowings collateralized by securities as an alternative source of funds.  These funds are available through the broker dealer community.  However, the Bank realizes the risks inherent in repurchase agreement transactions.  To mitigate these risks, the Bank:



Maintains established written credit policies;



Requires identification and periodic credit evaluations of each counterparty;



Establishes maximum position and exposure limits for each counterparty;



Mandates individual or master written agreements for all repurchase transactions that specify acceptable collateral types and maturities, call defaults and sellout provisions, ownership rights, substitute collateral rights, and persons authorized to transact business on behalf of both parties; and



Provide for acceptable control provisions over underlying securities.



It is the policy of the Bank regarding a plan to engage in the sale of repurchase agreements to retail customers to consult with legal counsel competent in the field of securities law to determine what constitutes sufficient disclosure to customers in addition to ensuring compliance with the antifraud, and other applicable provisions of federal and state securities law.  Repo lines may be established with Board of Directors approved brokerage firms and banks in accordance with the Bank’s VENDOR MANAGEMENT PROGRAM POLICY.



Dollar Repurchase Agreements.  Also known as dollar repos and dollar rolls, these instruments provide the Bank with an alternative method of borrowing against securities owned.  Unlike "standard" repurchase agreements, dollar repos require the buyer to return to the seller substantially similar, versus identical, securities.  Dealers typically offer dollar roll financing to the Bank as a means of covering short positions in particular securities.  Short positions arise when a dealer sells securities that it does not currently own for forward delivery.  To avoid the costs associated with failing on a delivery, dealers are willing to offer attractive financing rates in exchange for the use of the Bank's securities in covering a short position.



To qualify as borrowings, dollar repos must require the buyer to return to the seller "substantially similar" securities by the settlement date, which cannot exceed 12 months from the inception of the transaction.  Mortgage pass through securities repurchased are considered "substantially similar" to those sold if all of the following conditions are met:



Are collateralized with similar mortgages;



Are issued by the same agency and be part of the same program;



Have the same remaining weighted average maturity;



Are priced to have similar market yields;



Have identical coupon rates; and



Satisfy good delivery requirements.



In addition, securities used in dollar repo transactions must have been held in the seller's investment portfolio for a minimum of 35 consecutive days prior to the initiation of the contract.



Federal Reserve Bank Loans.  The Federal Reserve Bank can provide the Bank with short term collateralized credit via its discount window.  The Federal Reserve Bank will extend primary credit at a rate above the target Fed Funds rate on a short term basis (typically, overnight) to eligible depository institutions.  The Federal Reserve Bank bases eligibility for primary credit on an institution’s examination rating and capital status.  In general, adequately capitalized institutions with composite CAMELS ratings of 1, 2, or 3 are eligible for primary credit unless supplementary information indicates their condition is not generally sound.  Other conditions exist to determine eligibility for 4 and 5 rated institutions.



An institution eligible for primary credit need not exhaust other sources of funds before coming to the discount window.  Institutions may use primary credit to finance the sale of fed funds.  However, because of the above market price of primary credit, the Federal Reserve Board expects institutions to mainly use the discount window as a backup source of liquidity, rather than as a routine source.



Generally, the Federal Reserve Bank will extend primary credit on an overnight basis with minimal administrative requirements to eligible institutions, and also may extend primary credit to eligible institutions for periods of up to several weeks if funding is not available from other sources.  These longer extensions of credit are subject to greater administrative oversight.



In addition, the Federal Reserve Bank also offers secondary credit to institutions that do not qualify for primary credit.  Secondary credit is typically another short term backup source of liquidity.  Long term secondary credit would be available for the orderly resolution of a troubled institution.  In such a case, there are certain limitations and a higher level of Federal Reserve Bank administration and oversight.



The Bank has executed borrowing agreements and fully collateralizes all borrowings.  U.S. government securities are the most acceptable and most common type of collateral in obtaining a loan, although any "bankable" asset is acceptable for pledging.  Other acceptable collateral consists of mortgage backed, asset backed, municipal, sovereign, or corporate securities, and loans (municipal, commercial, 1-4 family residential).  Collateral may be transferred to the Federal Reserve Bank, held by the borrower in custody, held by a third party, or reflected by book entry.



Brokered and Rate Sensitive Deposits



The term "brokered deposit" means any deposit that is obtained, directly or indirectly, from or through the mediation or assistance of a deposit broker.  However, brokered deposits do not include those deposits obtained by a listing service that meets the following criteria:



The person or entity providing the listing service is compensated solely by means of subscription fees (i.e., the fees paid by subscribers as payment for their opportunity to see the rates gathered by the listing service) and/or listing fees (i.e., the fees paid by the insured depository institution as payment for the opportunity to list or "post" their rates).  The listing service does not require an insured depository institution to pay for other services offered by the listing service or its affiliates as a condition precedent to being listed.



The fees paid by an insured depository institution are flat fees, in which they are not calculated based on the number or dollar amount of deposits accepted by the insured depository institution as a result of the listing of the insured depository institution’s rates.



In exchange for fees, the listing service performs no service except the gathering and transmission of information concerning the availability of deposits.  This information may include an insured depository institution's name, address (including e-mail address), telephone number and interest rates.  Except for providing this information, the listing service does not serve as a liaison between depositors and an insured depository institution.  For example, the listing service does not pass information about a depositor to an insured depository institution.



The listing service is not involved in placing deposits or confirming the placement of deposits.  Any funds to be invested in deposit accounts are remitted directly by the depositor to the insured depository institution and not, directly or indirectly, by or through the listing service.



The term “deposit broker” means:



Any person engaged in the business of placing deposits, or facilitating the placement of deposits, of third parties with insured depository institutions, or the business of placing deposits with insured depository institutions for the purpose of selling interests in those deposits to third parties; and



An agent or trustee who establishes a deposit account to facilitate a business arrangement with an insured depository institution to use the proceeds of the account to fund a prearranged loan.



The term also includes any insured depository institution that is not well capitalized, and any employee of any such insured depository institution, which engages, directly or indirectly, in the solicitation of deposits by offering rates of interest (with respect to such deposits) which are significantly higher than the prevailing rates of interest on deposits offered by other insured depository institutions in such depository institution's normal market area.

The term does not include:



An insured depository institution, with respect to funds placed with that depository institution;



An employee of an insured depository institution, with respect to funds placed with the employing depository institution;



A trust department of an insured depository institution, if the trust or other fiduciary relationship in question has not been established for the primary purpose of placing funds with insured depository institutions;



The trustee of a pension or other employee benefit plan, with respect to funds of the plan;



A person acting as a plan administrator or an investment adviser in connection with a pension plan or other employee benefit plan provided that person is performing managerial functions with respect to the plan;



The trustee of a testamentary account;

The following represents capitalization categories with respect to brokered deposit guidelines in accordance with Section 38 of the Federal Deposit Insurance Act and Part 337.6 of the FDIC’s Rules and Regulations:



Well Capitalized.  A well capitalized insured depository institution may solicit and accept, renew or roll over any brokered deposit without restriction.



Adequately Capitalized.  An adequately capitalized insured depository institution may not accept, renew or roll over any brokered deposit unless it has applied for and been granted a waiver of this prohibition by the FDIC.  Any adequately capitalized insured depository institution that has been granted a waiver to accept, renew or roll over a brokered deposit may not pay an effective yield on any such deposit which, at the time that such deposit is accepted, renewed or rolled over, exceeds by more than 75 basis points:



The effective yield paid on deposits of comparable size and maturity in such institution's normal market area for deposits accepted from within its normal market area; or



The national rate paid on deposits of comparable size and maturity for deposits accepted outside the institution's normal market area.  For purposes of this provision, the national rate shall be a simple average of rates paid by all insured depository institutions and branches for which data are available.  This rate shall be determined by the FDIC.



Undercapitalized.  An undercapitalized insured depository institution may not accept, renew or roll over any brokered deposit.  An undercapitalized insured depository institution may not solicit deposits by offering an effective yield that exceeds by more than 75 basis points the prevailing effective yields on insured deposits of comparable maturity in such institution's normal market area or in the market area in which such deposits are being solicited.



Waiver.  The FDIC may, on a case by case basis and upon application by an adequately capitalized insured depository institution, waive the prohibition on the acceptance, renewal or rollover of brokered deposits upon a finding that such acceptance, renewal or roll over does not constitute an unsafe or unsound practice with respect to such institution.  The FDIC may conclude that it is not unsafe or unsound and may grant a waiver when the acceptance, renewal or rollover of brokered deposits is determined to pose no undue risk to the institution.  Any waiver granted may be revoked at any time by written notice to the institution.



Exclusion for Institutions in FDIC Conservatorship.  No insured depository institution for which the FDIC has been appointed conservator shall be subject to the prohibition on the acceptance, renewal or rollover of brokered deposits or Section 29 Federal Deposit Insurance Act for 90 days after the date on which the institution was placed in conservatorship.  During this 90 day period, the institution shall, nevertheless, be subject to the restriction on the payment of interest.  After such 90 day period, the institution may not accept, renew or roll over any brokered deposit.



Market.  A market is any readily defined geographical area in which the rates offered by any one insured depository institution soliciting deposits in that area may affect the rates offered by other insured depository institutions operating in the same area.  The effective yield on a deposit with an odd maturity shall be determined by interpolating between the yields offered by other insured depository institutions on deposits of the next longer and shorter maturities offered in the market.



It is presumed that the prevailing rate or effective yield in the relevant market is the national rate, unless the FDIC determines in its sole discretion based on available evidence that the effective yield in that market differs from the national rate.  Evidence of the effective yield in a particular market may include (but is not limited to) the following criteria:



Rates paid by other insured depository institutions in the same state, county or metropolitan statistical area (though the FDIC shall not be obligated to recognize each area as a separate market area);



Rates paid by credit unions in the same market area if the FDIC determines that the insured depository institution competes directly with these credit unions; and



Different rates paid on different deposit products in the same market area (though the FDIC shall not be obligated to recognize all alleged distinctions among various deposit products).  As an example for a particular market, evidence exists that the rates on money market deposit accounts (MMDAs) differ from the rates on negotiable order of withdrawal (NOW) accounts.  MMDAs are distinguishable from NOW accounts in that the two types of accounts are subject to different legal requirements.  Under these circumstances for this market, the FDIC could recognize that the prevailing rate on MMDAs is different than the prevailing rate on NOW accounts.

The following outlines the Bank’s basic loan pricing methodologies:



Floating Rates and Fixed Rates with Maturities of Less Than One Year.  As the majority, normally at least PERCENT of the Bank’s loan portfolio will be rate sensitive.  Rates will be set at a fixed amount over the Bank’s Base Rate (which normally would equal the “Wall Street Journal” Prime Rate).  Most floating rate loans should have a “floor” to protect against a great fall in rates.  Many loans will have a ceiling as well, and the level of the ceiling will vary with maturity (the longer the term, the higher the ceiling).  The actual pricing will be set by the approving authority (Senior Management and the Board of Directors’ Loan Committee).  Lower floors may be necessary in times of lower rates, but such lower floors may be allowed when necessary to maintain a competitive position in the loan market.

Fixed Rates.  Even with the rate fluctuations over recent years, some fixed rate lending is prudent, and in fact can, under certain circumstances, greatly enhance profitability.  With regards to fixed rate lending:



Amounts extended and maturity dates should not be considered as to periods of time.  The Bank should straddle the market as to rates and maturities.  TIME PERIOD is normally the longest maturity considered with TIME PERIOD the maximum and only for exceptional high quality customers.  Occasionally, longer terms may be necessary to sell repossessed real estate.



Fixed rate loans should be priced at premium over what a comparable floating rate loan would be.  For example, if the base rate is 10% and a base plus 2% is appropriate, then a fixed rate higher that 12% should be set with the rate going higher with longer maturities.



Prepayment penalties and larger loan fees may also be appropriate with fixed rate term loans.



Consideration of whether rates are in a “rising” or falling” trend may be important in addition to whether rates are relatively “high” or “low.”  However, the volume of fixed rate lending is to be limited and the Bank’s approach to fixed rate lending should remain NON SPECULATIVE.

It is the policy of the Bank to implement and maintain an effective Contingency Funding Plan (CFP) that identifies maximum and minimum liquidity needs and weighs alternative courses of action designed to address these needs in emergency situations.  The Bank realizes that funding decisions can be influenced by unplanned events and unanticipated stressful scenarios that can result in a significant erosion of Bank specific or general market liquidity.  Such events include, but are not limited to, the:



Inability to fund asset growth;



Difficulty renewing or replacing maturing funding liabilities;



Customers unexpectedly exercising options to withdraw deposits, draw down lines of credit, or exercise off-balance sheet commitments;



Changes in market value and price volatility of various asset types;



Changes in economic conditions, market perception, or dislocations in the financial markets;



Disturbances in payment and settlement systems due to operational or local disasters;



Legal or operational risks; and



Demise of a business line.



The Bank’s CFP is part of its comprehensive liquidity risk management program that is designed to address alternative funding if initial projections of funding sources and uses are incorrect or if a liquidity crisis arises.  This strategy can be adopted quickly, and is a cash flow projection and comprehensive funding plan that forecasts funding needs and funding sources under market scenarios including aggressive asset growth or rapid liability erosion.  The plan represents the Bank’s best estimate of balance sheet changes that may result from a liquidity or credit event, or other adverse scenarios to prudently and effectively manage routine and extraordinary fluctuations in liquidity.  The plan also helps management monitor liquidity risk, ensure that an appropriate amount of liquid assets is maintained, measure and project funding requirements during various scenarios, and manage access to funding sources.  Potential scenarios that may affect the Bank’s liquidity position include a decline in revenue due to such factors as a prolonged period in which investment prices fall, the loss of a key officer, or the loss of a significant account relationship.  Litigation, fraud, and other potential impairments can also have a material impact on the Bank’s liquidity needs.  In addition, the plan can help control day to day liquidity risk by showing that the Bank, even if it is in financial trouble, can find sources of funds to cover its funding needs.



The Bank reviews its funding position if the CFP predicts more uses of funds than sources in a near term scenario.  To reduce funding and liquidity risks, the Bank generally either:



Replaces credit sensitive liabilities with more stable, credit insensitive funding such as long term borrowing or retail deposits; or



Reduces assets that require term funding, such as loans.



This plan helps ensure the Bank can prudently and efficiently manage routine and extraordinary fluctuations in liquidity.

9.	INTEREST RATE RISK POLICY REFERENCE



General



It is the policy of the Bank to implement and maintain an Interest Rate Risk Program that is commensurate with the Bank’s earnings and capital levels, complexity, business model, risk profile, and scope of operations.  The Bank realizes that effective interest rate risk (IRR) management processes are particularly important in times when the Bank might experience downward pressure on earnings and capital due to lower credit quality and market illiquidity.



In general, IRR is the risk to the Bank’s capital or earnings derived from adverse changes in interest rates and results from:



Repricing Risk.  Repricing risk results from timing differences between coupon changes or cash flows from assets, liabilities, and off-balance sheet instruments.  For example, long term fixed rate securities funded by short term deposits may create repricing risk.  If interest rates change, then deposit funding costs will change more quickly than the yield on the securities.  Likewise, the present value of the securities (i.e., their market price) will change more than the value of the deposits, thereby affecting the value of capital.



Basis Risk.  Basic risk results from weak correlation between coupon rate changes for assets, liabilities, and off-balance sheet instruments.  For instance, LIBOR based deposit rates may change by 50 basis points, while prime based loan rates may change by only 25 basis points during the same period.  Basis risk originates from the potential for market differences when a position denominated in one currency (USD) is used to offset an exposure marked to another (Euro).



Yield Curve Risk.  Yield curve risk results from changing rate relationships between different maturities of the same index.  For example, a 30 year Treasury bond’s yield may change by 200 basis points, but a three year Treasury note’s yield may change by only 50 basis points during the same period.



Option Risk.  Option risk results when a financial instrument’s cash flow timing or amount can change as a result of a decision exercised by a borrowing or lending counterparty, typically in response to market interest rate changes.  This can adversely affect earnings by reducing asset yields or increasing funding costs, and it may reduce the net present value of expected cash flows.



Changes in interest rates affect the Bank's earnings by changing its net interest income and the level of other interest sensitive income and operating expenses.  Changes in interest rates also affect the underlying economic value of the Bank's assets, liabilities and off-balance sheet instruments because the present value of future cash flows and in some cases, the cash flows themselves, change when interest rates change.  The combined effects of the changes in these present values reflect the change in the Bank's underlying economic value.  The Bank’s Interest Rate Risk Program, which includes this policy and subsequent procedures and processes, is specifically tailored to meet these unique needs and circumstances as an essential component of its safe and sound banking practices.



Interest Rate Risk Program Overview



The Bank realizes that effective control of IRR requires a comprehensive risk management process that ensures the timely identification, measurement, monitoring, and control of risk.  The formality and sophistication of these elements are based upon the level of the Bank's risk and the complexity of its holdings and activities.  In general terms, the Bank’s Interest Rate Risk Program, which is incorporated into the Bank’s overall Risk Management Program, includes the following key risk management elements to meet these objectives:



Responsibility and Authority.  The Bank has implemented policies, procedures and processes designed to control the nature and amount of IRR the Bank takes including those that specify risk limits and define lines of responsibilities and authority for managing risk and authorizing policy exceptions.



Interest Rate Risk Measurement System.  The Bank has implemented a system for identifying and measuring IRR that enables the Bank to identify and quantify the major sources of the Bank’s IRR in a timely manner.



Monitoring and Reporting Risk Exposure System.  The Bank has implemented a system for monitoring and reporting risk exposures which enables the Board of Directors and Senior Management to receive reports on the Bank’s IRR profile at least quarterly, but more frequently if the character and level of the Bank’s risk requires.  These reports are to be designed to allow the Board of Directors and Senior Management to evaluate the amount of IRR being taken, compliance with established risk limits, and whether the Bank’s strategies are appropriate in light of the Board of Directors’ expressed risk tolerance.



Risk Controls and Limits.  The Bank has implemented a system of risk controls and limits regarding the nature and amount of IRR that can be taken.  When determining risk exposure limits, it is the responsibility of the ALCO to consider the nature of the Bank’s strategies and activities, its past performance, the level of earnings and capital available to absorb potential losses, and the Board of Directors’ tolerance for risk.



In general, it is the responsibility of the Board of Directors and Senior Management to ensure that the structure of the Bank's business and the level of IRR it assumes are effectively managed and that appropriate policies, practices and processes are established to control and limit risks by delineating clear lines of responsibility and authority for the following areas:



Identifying the potential IRR arising from existing or new products or activities;



Establishing and maintaining an IRR measurement system;



Formulating and executing strategies to manage IRR exposures; and



Authorizing policy exceptions.



As stated earlier in this policy, the ALCO is responsible for managing this process with appropriate oversight by the Board of Directors.  Subsequent topics and sections of the policy identify the types of instruments and activities that the Bank uses to manage its IRR exposure, including permissible instruments (either specifically or by their characteristics) and the purposes or objectives for which they are used.  In addition to this policy, and other related policies, procedures and processes, it also delineates procedures for acquiring specific instruments, managing portfolios, and controlling the Bank's aggregate IRR exposure.



Internal Controls.  The Bank has implemented a system of internal controls, reviews and audits to ensure the integrity of the overall risk management process.  Oversight by the Board of Directors and Senior Management is critical to the internal control process.  In addition to establishing clear lines of authority, responsibilities, and risk limits, it is the responsibility of the Board of Directors and Senior Management to ensure that adequate resources are provided to support risk monitoring, audit, and control functions.  The persons or units responsible for risk monitoring and control functions are to be separate from the persons or units that create risk exposures.  The persons or units may be part of a more general operation, such as, audit, compliance, risk management, or treasury unit.  If the risk monitoring and control functions are part of a treasury unit that also has the responsibility and authority to execute investment or hedging strategies to manage the Bank’s risk exposure, it is particularly important that the Bank have a strong internal audit function and sufficient safeguards in place to ensure that all trades are reported to Senior Management in a timely manner and are consistent with strategies approved by Senior Management.

The basic objectives of the investment function are:



To keep the Bank’s funds fully employed at the maximum after tax return;



To minimize exposure to credit risk; and



To provide liquidity required by current circumstances.



The term “liquidity” used in this policy primarily refers to the expected cash flows from performing assets and secondarily to borrowings secured by performing assets.  These two sources of liquidity have in the past been sufficient to fund the operations of the Bank, and the Board of Directors anticipates that they will suffice in the future.  For this reason, unless otherwise indicated, the term liquidity in this policy does not refer to proceeds from the sale of assets, except for the sale of assets available for sale.

Preservation of principal;



Strong cash flow characteristics;



Ready availability of credit information;



Appropriateness of size both as to the Bank and as to an obligor’s outstanding debt;



Eligibility as collateral for public agency deposits and customer repurchase agreement contracts; and



Broad marketability, as an indicator of quality.



The Bank reserves and allocates its funding base of shareholders’ equity and non-interest bearing liabilities for the purchase and carrying of tax advantaged investments such as bank qualified municipal securities.



In general, the Bank's investment portfolio can provide liquidity through maturing securities, the sale of securities for cash, or by pledging securities as collateral in a repurchase agreement or other hypothecation.  For an investment to be sold or pledged as collateral, it must not be presently encumbered.  That is, the security cannot be pledged, used as collateral, sold under repurchase agreement, or otherwise hypothecated.  Even if unencumbered, a security that is severely depreciated, has a small face amount, or is of poor credit quality is not a good candidate for collateral.



For accounting purposes, the Bank’s investment portfolios are separated into three categories:



Held to Maturity (HTM);



Available for Sale (AFS); and



Trading.



Securities categorized as HTM are carried at amortized cost.  To categorize a security as HTM, the Bank must have both the intent and ability to hold the security to maturity.  If the Bank has any intention of selling a HTM security prior to maturity for liquidity purposes, the security is not eligible for classification as HTM.  Sale of a HTM security could potentially call into question the Bank’s ability to hold other HTM securities to maturity.  In this respect, a reclassification might be required of the remaining HTM securities (categorizing them as AFS or trading).  In addition, the Bank would have difficulty categorizing future securities purchases as HTM.  However, HTM securities can be pledged or used as collateral in a repurchase agreement or other collateralized borrowing arrangements and provides the Bank with a source of liquidity.  Furthermore, in situations when the Bank needs cash immediately, management might ignore the accounting ramifications of selling securities categorized as HTM rather than risk the Bank's viability.



The Bank generally classifies securities that will be used for liquidity as AFS.  AFS securities are not subject to the "intent and ability" restrictions of HTM securities.  Because AFS securities are marked to market regularly, any fair value gains or losses are recognized as they occur in a separate component of equity capital known as accumulated other comprehensive income.  Therefore, if the Bank needs to sell, pledge, or use an AFS security as collateral, the impact on GAAP capital is mitigated because the Bank has already recognized the change in value of the security.  However, since the unrealized gain or loss on AFS securities is not reflected in regulatory capital prior to a sale, there will be an impact on Tier 1 Capital when the AFS securities are sold.



The Bank’s only liquid investments are those which can be sold in an adverse market without significant losses.  First, all unpledged investments with maturities of less than TIME PERIOD are considered liquid.  Next, unpledged securities that can be demonstrated to be readily sellable or reliably used for pledging are deemed liquid.  Additionally, federal funds sold are PERCENT liquid.



In general, the Bank’s INVESTMENT POLICY determines:



Which types of investments are permitted;



The desired mix among those investments;



The maturity distribution and the amount of funds that will be available; and



Review pledging opportunities and requirements.

For example, after reviewing an amended Call Report, a bank’s main correspondent and clearing agent notifies the bank that it will no longer lend to the institution on an unsecured basis.  The bank is forced to enter into a repurchase agreement (using remaining unpledged securities) to ensure that the correspondent will continue to provide processing services.  Situations like these can result in a scramble for an alternative correspondent banking relationship at the least opportune time.

12.	AUDIT POLICY



The Bank has designated the Audit Officer and Audit Department to conduct an independent and periodic review of the Bank’s liquidity management process to ensure that an adequate system of internal controls in addition to compliance are maintained.  The review of such internal controls and compliance are to address relevant elements of the risk management process, including adherence to this and other policies, procedures and processes, the adequacy of risk identification, risk measurement, reporting, and compliance with all applicable rules, laws and regulations.  With the same respect, the Bank has engaged the services of an independent consultant to provide a comprehensive and independent audit review on a FREQUENCY basis.  This review is to assess the extent to which the Bank’s liquidity risk management complies with both supervisory guidance and industry sound practices, taking into account the level of sophistication and complexity of the Bank’s liquidity risk profile.



Results of the audit are reported to the Board of Directors Audit Committee, ALCO, and Senior Management.  It is the responsibility of the ALCO and Senior Management to take appropriate action to correct any exceptions found as a result of the audit.



13.	STAFF TRAINING



It is the responsibility of the Bank’s COMPLIANCE OFFICER to ensure that all members of the Board of Directors, Senior Management, ALCO, Liquidity Crisis Management Team, and the ACCOUNTING DEPARTMENT receive appropriate training on the Bank’s Asset, Liability and Liquidity Management Program and the directives of this policy on a FREQUENCY basis.  In addition, branch management and customer service personnel are to receive periodic training on how to communicate with depositors, including advising customers on how to properly title deposits and ensuring that they have an accurate understanding of their deposit insurance coverage.  All training is to be appropriately documented and placed in the Bank’s management training file for historical reference.



Personnel are responsible for the complete comprehension of this and other related policies and procedures, including a complete understanding of their own specific responsibilities.  The COMPLIANCE OFFICER and/or other designated members are to periodically attend educational programs related to asset and liability management.



NOTE:  If at any time an employee has difficulty with a customer or is uncertain about the proper method of handling a situation or transaction, he or she should refer the issue to their immediate supervisor or contact the COMPLIANCE OFFICER for further clarification.



14.	RETENTION OF DOCUMENTATION



For archive purposes the organization, storage and scanning of documentation related to the Bank’s Asset, Liability and Liquidity Management Program is the responsibility of the ACCOUNTING DEPARTMENT.  Files are maintained in single custody during banking hours and are locked in a fire protected location at night.  All documentation (initially or when new documents are added) within each file is scanned by ACCOUNTING DEPARTMENT personnel for permanent archive and research purposes.



Outdated documentation files (and scanned archive media) are periodically sent to storage with a destruction date of TIME PERIOD from the date of shipment.  A listing of all files sent to storage is maintained by the ACCOUNTING DEPARTMENT.
