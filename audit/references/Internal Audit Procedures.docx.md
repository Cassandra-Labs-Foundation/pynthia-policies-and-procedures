  
Internal Audit Procedures  
The Bank

1. Frequency. Scope and Scheduling \- It is Bank policy that the auditors submit a yearly general audit scope and frequency schedule and risk assessment to the Board of Directors (Audit Committee) for approval. The schedule will include an explanation of the scope of audit activities for the year and the frequencies in which specific audits will be performed.

Audits will be tentatively scheduled monthly and then finalized. The purpose of scheduling audits this way is to provide flexibility in scheduling in the event an audit takes more or less time than was expected. When possible, audits will be planned to minimize conflict with the work of the compliance/operational function of the bank. 

The audit frequency and scope are not strict policies. Instead, they are guidelines and are subject to revision. The scope of each audit should be reviewed before the commencement of that audit. If, during the review, major changes in the operational function to be audited are discovered, the auditor will determine if the changes will affect the scope of the audit and, if so, make appropriate schedule changes. Changes in the scope of the audit may also be necessary during the performance of the audit, if the following situations occur:

* The results of audit tests cannot be accurately measured, and the nature of the exceptions disclosed requires further investigation.  
* When a change in policies renders current audit techniques meaningless. 

After an audit is completed, the overall condition of the department, as determined by the audit, should provide a basis for determining if a change in audit frequency is appropriate. For example, if internal controls in the compliance/operational function were found to be very weak, the auditor would consider shortening the audit frequency until controls are improved. On the other hand, if initial controls were found to be strong and no material exceptions or errors were found, the auditor would consider lengthening the audit frequency. The cost of auditing must be weighed against the risk of material errors, irregularities, and losses occurring in the operations function. All audit frequency and scope changes will be documented in the audit work papers. The documentation will include a full explanation of the circumstances and reasons for the change in audit frequency and scope.

2. Pre-Audit Preparations \- Proper administration and pre-audit preparation are two keys to a successful and cost-effective audit. Without careful planning and organizing of audit work, it is probable that adequate audit coverage will not be provided or that an excessive amount of time and money will be spent to complete the audit.

3. Initial Control Questionnaire \- The initial control questionnaire is designed to aid the auditor in evaluating adherence to policies, practices, and initial controls. The questionnaire is designed so that "no" answers indicate a control weakness might exist. A formal questionnaire may not be used for all audits, however there is always some form of questions that the auditor will use in the evaluation process. 

In completing the questionnaire, the auditor will use several sources of information to satisfy her/himself that an adequate control exists. The auditor may use inquiries, observation, or testing of records to obtain the information necessary to complete the questionnaire.  

Often, supporting documentation is available in the form of written policies, operating instructions, job descriptions, procedures manuals, directives, flow charts, etc., which can be copied or referenced to the work papers to substantiate the existence of a control. Inquiries to substantiate the existence of a control are discouraged. If needed, an explanation of the circumstances should be provided in the work papers.  

As previously mentioned, a "no" answer indicates that a control weakness may exist. The auditor must satisfy her/himself that other alternatives or compensating controls do not exist before a "no" answer is entered in the questionnaire. The circumstances and consideration of any compensating controls should be documented and referred to on the questionnaire. If it is determined that a weakness does exist, it should be documented. The auditor must also consider the need for a change in the audit program due to nonexistence of a control. Different audit tests or additional testing may be required to satisfy him/herself that no irregularities or errors have occurred due to a lack of control. 

Once the questionnaire is completed, the auditor must determine the overall condition of the audit program and review it to determine if it adequately tests controls and practices in use. Consideration must be given to the control conditions. 

4. Audit Program \- The audit program defines audit procedures to be executed during the audit. Each procedure on the program will be refined to on the applicable work papers. The program steps are directly related to internal control, operating procedures, and compliance of the area under audit and, therefore, are subject to revision as these procedures change. As was previously mentioned, audit coverage (scope) varies from audit to audit to provide adequate internal auditing at minimum cost while considering loss exposure to the Bank. Several factors must be reviewed in determining if the audit program for a particular function provides adequate coverage, or if a scope change is appropriate. The following factors should be evaluated to determine if the audit program includes procedures for adequate coverage:

* The operation under audit should be reviewed to determine if the audit program for the activity includes procedures for testing all significant bank policies, new and old, and if policy changes have occurred since the previous audit.

* Any legal and regulatory changes for the operation under audit should be reviewed, and the audit program updated.

* Is the auditor aware of any other conditions or changes in the operation that could have an effect on the scope of the audit? Some examples of such conditions are excessive employee turnover, employee dismissals, excessive errors in records, or tardiness of departmental work. Such conditions are red flags to the auditor. They suggest that controls are weak or that unusual or irregular activities exist.

* The auditor should determine if any new banking services (which should be audited) have been implemented in the operation since the previous audit. 

If the review disclosed that a change in the audit scope is required, appropriate changes in the audit program must be made to provide for adequate coverage. 

An internal control questionnaire may be used in connection with the audit. The relationship may be thought of as follows: the questionnaire provides an inventory of the controls present in the area under audit which documents the operating procedures; the audit program provides audit procedures to test the adequacy of the control procedures, to detect any errors or irregularities, and to determine compliance with laws, regulations, and Bank policies and procedures. Thus, the conclusions drawn, as a result of the questionnaire may indicate a change needed in the audit program. This should be documented in the work papers.

Just before the audit, the sample sizes of records to be detail-tested should be determined. If, in the auditor's opinion, an audit procedure has uncovered an unusual number of errors or irregularities of an unusual nature, the extent of the testing should be expanded, and additional audit procedures may be required. This would result in a change in the audit program. In such circumstances, this should be documented in the work papers. 

5. Previous Audit Follow-up \- Previous internal audit, bank examination, and external audit reports should be reviewed for recommendations that were made for correcting exceptions and improving internal controls. During the current audit, a follow-up of corrective action taken on the recommendations should be performed.

6. Items to be Controlled \- The auditor must plan for sealing and controlling various items from the start of the audit. It is imperative that the auditor control and restrict access by operational personnel to items such as cash drawers, cash vault, collateral file, traveler’s checks, commercial notes, etc., until they have been verified by audit personnel. Careful planning for items to be controlled will ensure that the items and records are not contaminated before audit verification is completed.

7. Conducting the Audit \- The primary objectives of the audit is to determine whether or not adequate internal controls exist and if prescribed policies, laws and regulations are being followed. To achieve these objectives, the auditor must gather and document corroborative evidence to support audit findings and conclusions. While conducting an audit, the corroborative evidence is gathered by three basic means:

* Inquiry \- The internal control questionnaire is the most common form of inquiry used by the auditor. Internal control questionnaires are used to question operational personnel about control procedures, policies and activities.

* Observation \- Through observation, the auditor can determine whether or not control procedures are being performed by operational personnel and whether policies, laws and regulations are being followed.

* Verification \- Another means of gathering evidence is through audit verification procedures. Basically, verification procedures consist of reviewing documents containing evidence that polices exist and that control procedures, policies, laws, and regulations are being followed. It also consists of testing records for completeness and accuracy and confirming balances with third parties.

The auditor may use one or more of these evidence-gathering methods to reach a conclusion. In any event, the auditor must gather sufficient corroborative evidence to satisfy him/herself and others that his/her findings and conclusions are accurate.

The auditor will communicate findings with the department supervisor throughout the audit process. The auditor will take into consideration any corrections/improvements the department makes during the audit, however all findings will be documented and reported in the audit report.

8. Audit Work Papers \- Audit work papers provide a permanent record of work performed and support for audit reports. Work papers should document the scope of the audit, sources of the information and documents reviewed, the audit findings, and the conclusions.

Audit work papers will be prepared in accordance with the following criteria:

* Work Paper Headings \- To help identify each work paper, headings will contain the following information:  
  * Name of the bank  
  * Type of audit  
  * Audit date  
  * Name of the person performed by   
  * Testing objective  
  * Method of sample (e.g. statistical or judgmental)  
  * Selecting and justifying a representative sample sufficient to support conclusions  
  * Description of sample

* Content of Work Papers \- Each work paper should contain the following information:

 

* Description of subject matter examined  
  * The source of the information  
  * The audit procedures, techniques, and methods used, and the extent of the examination   
  * Summary of findings  
  * Auditor's conclusion concerning the review

* Audit Documents Work Papers \- Audit documents are to be used to summarize audit work necessary to supplement the related work papers. Some uses are the following:

  * To document pertinent observations made while performing various tests.  
  * To summarize the results and conclusions of several tests in a given area which are documented on several work papers.

In general, audit notes should be used any time pertinent information needs to be documented but is not specifically addressed in an audit program step or when it is felt that a summary is needed to provide clarity concerning audit procedures, results, or conclusions.

* Audit Completion and Wrap-up \- After the audit field work is completed, the auditor will review and discuss the overall findings of the audit with the department supervisor. The auditor will obtain management responses if applicable from the department supervisor. The objectives of the wrap-up are as follows:

  * Determine that all necessary audit procedures were completed.  
  * Determine that the audit findings are accurate and that no further audit work should be required.  
  * Review audit work papers to determine that all audit work and findings are documented.  
  * Determine what corrective measures are to be discussed during the exit interview with management.  
  * Establish who the responsible party will be for the corrective action.   
  * Establish a reasonable time frame with the department supervisor for the implementation date of the audit findings.

* Audit Exit Interview \- When possible, the auditor will prepare a rough draft (preliminary) audit report and use it to conduct the exit interview. If this is not practical, they should use the exception listings that were prepared during the audit wrap-up. While conducting the exit interview, the responses of the supervisor/management will be recorded for documentation and for future reference purposes if not already provided. Copies of the report or exceptions listings will be given to the interviewer at this time. The objectives of the exit interview are the following:

  * To discuss with managers and supervisors the audit findings and recommendations for corrective action.  
  * To give each supervisor an opportunity to respond to the findings before his or her supervisor reviews them.  
  * To provide the auditor feedback to the findings and recommendations.  
    * The feedback should give the auditor a good idea about the supervisor's attitude towards pursuing corrective actions.  
    * The feedback should provide the auditor with an indication of the correctness of exceptions and deficiencies and the appropriateness of corrective recommendations. The auditor should carefully consider any objections.  
    * These might demonstrate that an audit finding is erroneous or that a recommendation is not appropriate. The auditor will require, however, that the supervisor provide proof of the objections to the auditor's satisfaction before findings and recommendations are changed.

9. Reporting \- Accurate and timely reporting of audit findings is quite important to an effective audit function. Without effective reporting, the purpose of performing audits is defeated. The department must be able to communicate audit results to management in writing. The audit report should include the following points:   
* Communication of accurate audit findings and meaningful recommendations to management on a timely basis.  
* Provide management the information necessary to take corrective action and to make effective decisions.  
* Provide management with an independent option concerning the condition of the operational functions for which they are responsible.  
* Formally document audit findings.  
* Report to management the extent of audit work performed.  
* Provide a risk rating of the overall audit findings and individual findings. 

* Report Content \- Reports should be written in terminology familiar to the readers and should effectively communicate findings so that minimum effort is required of the readers. In most cases, this is achieved by reporting only exceptions. This is the format in which audit reports should be written, and the reader should be able to easily determine the exceptions and findings that are important. Reports should be kept as short as possible while communicating the audit results. Reports should contain only facts.

* Report Format \- It is Bank policy that audit reports be prepared in a format which includes the following sections:  
  * Cover sheet identifying the audit and the audit date  
  * Table of Contents  
  * Background   
  * Audit Scope  
  * Overall Assessment and Risk Rating  
  * Findings and Individual Risk Ratings  
  * Recommendations  
  * Root Cause  
  * Management Responses  
  * Responsible Party and Implementation Date  
  * Detailed Engagement Scope  
      
* Introductory Paragraph \- The introductory paragraph will provide the reader with a brief indication of the content of the remainder of the report and will disclose the opening and closing dates of audit. It will indicate the significance of the findings that follow and the urgency of management action required. The general conditions which existed in the operational function under audit during the time of the audit will be disclosed.  
    
  After reading the introductory paragraph, the reader should have a general understanding of the significance of the findings and an indication as to the amount of concern he/she should have with the details of the report.  
    
* Scope \- The audit report should include a concise statement of the scope of the audit. The scope should convey to the reader the verification and testing procedures used and to what extent they were performed.


  When properly defined, the scope will give the reader a general understanding of the extent of the work performed in completing the audit. When defining the scope, avoid merely describing the audit. Any audit work that was limited must be disclosed in the scope. For instance, if audit work was limited to a review of records from the auditor through September, this should be stated.  
    
* Findings \- The findings section of the report will be limited to major exceptions and irregularities, and a separate paragraph section should be written for each one. It should be noted that determining which exceptions are significant and warrant reporting and which are not is, in most cases, a difficult task. It requires a considerable amount of judgment and experience to become proficient at these decisions. A good rule of thumb is: if there is a question as to the significance of an exception, it should be included in the report for management's consideration.

  In the discussion of findings, the circumstances of the exception or irregularity will be explained. In addition, the auditor will indicate the extent to which exceptions, irregularities, or errors exist. It is not necessary to discuss details of the findings, but it is important that the significance of the findings be clearly stated. The most important facts concerning the findings are the causes of the exceptions or irregularities. Procedural weakness or the lack of procedures, which has the potential of resulting in exceptions or irregularities, should also be reported as a finding. The fact that an exception was found is significant; however, more important is that the auditor determined the causes of the exceptions and disclosed these causes in the report. Unless cause is determined, proper corrective action is not possible and recommendations for preventative measures probably will not be effective.

  When reporting findings, report only facts. Otherwise the auditor will lose credibility, and the audit function will become ineffective.

  For every corrective situation finding that action and is discussed, the auditor must indicate the necessary make a specific recommendation to remedy the

  The corrective action recommendations should consist of the immediate action needed to correct the exceptions or irregularities which were discovered as a result of the audit. When possible, the auditor should reach an agreement with the pertinent manager as to what corrective action is necessary and indicate the manager's agreement to the action in the report.

  Specific recommendations to remedy the cause of exceptions will generally consist of recommending preventive measures (controls) where insufficient controls are deemed to be the cause. However, in other cases, where controls are adequate but are being circumvented, a recommendation to enforce adherence to controls would be appropriate. When incompetent employees are the cause, a recommendation for training would probably be appropriate. In each case, the auditor must recommend specific action to remedy the cause of the exceptions, even when the recommended action is obvious.


* Audit Rating – An assessment of the finding for the Audit Committee and Management to quickly evaluate and focus on the risk to the bank and the importance of the findings in the audit.   
* Rating Standard   
  * High Risk Finding (Management Attention is Required / Needed)  
    * Potential Materiality / Regulatory / Legal Implications  
    * Critical Control Not Functioning or Non-Existent  
    * Weakness Undermines Integrity of System / Process  
    * Corporate Policies are Violated  
  * Moderate Risk Finding (Management Attention is Desirable)  
    * Impact is Isolated  
    * Control Issue is Mitigated by Other Controls  
    * Bank Saves Costs Through Implementing Improvements  
  * Low Risk Finding (Improvement Opportunities Only)  
    * Impact is Small and Limited  
    * Enhancement to Existing Controls Could Be Beneficial  
  * All findings are required to be formally reported to the Audit Committee during their quarterly meetings. Specific findings can be considered for discussion with the Audit Committee on an as-needed basis.  
      
* Recommendations – The recommendations of the audit will include any of the auditor's suggestions that would promote operational efficiency, effectiveness, and continued ongoing operations in the functional area under audit. These recommendations differ from recommendations in that they are not directed to correct an exception or error or to control weakness or deficiency.   Recommendations are to be directed to matters which may improve procedures, decrease expenses, increase profits, or increase management effectiveness to without changing the basic control structure that exists. 

  Recommendations are for management's consideration and may require any management action. The auditor can increase her/his credibility, effectiveness, and value to the Bank by making meaningful recommendations. Conversely, trivial, ill-considered or meaningless recommendations will seriously damage the auditor's credibility. Recommendations need to be carefully considered before they are added to the report.


* Root Cause – The analysis that consists of methods used to identify underlying cases of control deficiencies and also assist in the identification of thematic control issues. The internal auditor should apply the bank’s approved risk-scoring methodology when assigning the risk severity.


* Management Response – This section will include management’s response to the audit comments as heard by the audit department representative. Management will either respond to the risk and provide an action plan for remediation or accept the risk not later than 30 days from final report date. A remediation plan is a statement/summary that will include an implementation timeline, personnel involved to make the change, and processes/procedures that will be written to update/capture the new process. Internal Audit will then advise of a timeline for any necessary validation work as a follow-up to the implementation of the changes. Should the auditee choose to accept the risk, It should include all points that management is trying to make so that when the Audit Committee is reviewing the report they will have the most pertinent information available to them. The final audit report is distributed to management, the Board of Directors (Audit Committee), external auditors and examiners.  
    
* Responsible Party – The designated employee responsible for overall effective implementation of sound policies and procedures and for establishing and maintain internal controls for the audit finding(s).   
    
* Implementation Date – All corrective actions must be implemented prior to this date. Proof will be required for all corrective actions. 

* Reports Distribution \- Audit reports should be received by all persons who have managerial responsibilities for the function under audit, compliance committee/audit committee and by persons who will be directly affected by the results of the audit.

10. Audit Tracking \- Findings that arise from internal and external audits, and exam, are tracked, monitored, and followed up on with department supervisors, in a timely manner. Findings are reviewed at the Compliance Committee meeting on a monthly basis and findings older than 3 months are escalated to the Board of Directors (Audit Committee). Ownership of actions should be assigned to a supervisor level or above when completing reports. Tracking reports are reviewed by the Board of Directors monthly. 

11. Risk Acceptance \- In the event Management chooses to accept the risk associated with audit findings this must be fully documented in Audit Reporting and Tracking and provided to the Audit Committee (Board of Directors). In order to close these items Management must demonstrate that appropriate Governance protocols have been followed which in most cases will result in a requirement for Board level approval.  
      
12. Follow-Up Audits \- All identified Audit Findings are reviewed by Internal Audit prior to closure to ensure that management has remediated these appropriately.

Prior Audit Ratings are a driver for the annual risk assessment and a sufficiently poor audit result will thus necessitate enhanced audit review frequency.

13. Retention \- Work papers and audit reports must be retained seven years after the date of the audit report.

14. Physical Control \- Work papers are the property of the Internal Audit and should be kept under their control. The auditors should know exactly where the work papers are during the audit. When not in use, audit files should be kept in a secure location that is not readily available to persons not authorized to use them. If persons outside of the Internal Audit request to review the work papers, Audit Committee must approve the request.  
      
15. Development, Changing, and Implementation of Audit Work Programs – Audit Work Programs are developed, changed and implemented by using the regulatory audit manuals provided by the CFPB, OCC, FDIC, and Federal Reserve. Changed are made when there are updates to the regulatory guidance.
