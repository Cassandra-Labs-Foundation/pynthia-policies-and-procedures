**BSA/AML/CIP PROGRAM**

**Purpose**

The Bank Secrecy Act is intended to safeguard the U.S. financial system and the financial institutions that make up the system from the abuses of financial crime, including money laundering, terrorist financing, and other illicit financial transactions.  Money laundering and terrorist financing have the potential of devastating social and financial effects.

 Louisa Community Bank is committed to maintaining a high level of compliance with the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA).  As part of the Bank’s compliance with BSA, it has adopted an Anti-Money Laundering Program (AML) and Customer Identification Program (CIP).  The board of directors seeks to comply with laws and regulations and acknowledges the seriousness of the purposes involved.

The bank designed its program to achieve the following:

* A responsible position for coordinating and monitoring daily compliance.  
* A system of internal controls to assure ongoing compliance.  
* Independent and objective testing of compliance.  
* Training for appropriate personnel.  
* Risk-based procedures for conducting ongoing customer due diligence, to include, but not limited to:  
* Understanding the nature and purpose of customer relationships for the purpose of developing a customer risk profile; and  
* Conducting ongoing monitoring to identify and report suspicious transactions and, on a risk basis, to maintain and update customer information. Customer information will include information regarding the beneficial owners of legal entity customers.

An Assistant BSA compliance officer will also be designated to ensure continuity of the program.

**Risk Assessment**

A risk assessment will be conducted for the purpose of evaluating the bank’s exposure to being used for further illegal activity and terrorist financing. It will be updated and reported to the Board of Directors at least annually. If circumstances change dramatically, the assessment will be reviewed and revised more often than annually.   The assessment will encompass the bank’s asset size, products and services, customer base, and geographic locations.  The bank will establish related policies and procedures to meet legal requirements and to mitigate risks based on the risk assessment.  Procedures may be supplemented by additional steps as appropriate when determined appropriate by bank management.

The OFAC and Product/Services matrices are separate documents from the BSA/AML matrix and will be taken into consideration in the overall assessment.

* The bank will retain all identifying and verifying data obtained at account opening according to Section 326 of the US Patriot Act.  The required customer information will be retained for five years after the account is closed. Records used to verify identity, the methods and results of any measures undertaken to verify identity, and the resolution of any discrepancies discovered during verification will be retained for at least five years after the information is obtained.

Training should be tailored to a person’s or department’s responsibility or need.  All areas will receive training in the three stages of money laundering, terrorist financing, and suspicious activity.

* BSA Compliance Officer/Assistant BSA Officer will attend at least one external training session per year.  It should be relevant and appropriate to any changes to regulatory requirements or the overall BSA/AML risk profile of the bank.    
    
* Board of Directors will receive annual training.  This should include but not be limited to: the importance of regulatory requirements, the consequences of noncompliance, and risks posed to the bank.

* New employees will receive initial training within 30 days from the hire date.

Training records and materials will be maintained.  Training sessions will be reported to the audit committee or Board of Directors.

Independent, objective testing of compliance with BSA/AML laws and regulations and bank policy and procedures will be performed at least once annually.  Persons performing independent tests will report directly to the Board of Directors or the audit committee.  

The independent review should address the following:

* The overall integrity and effectiveness of the BSA/AML/CIP compliance program, including policies, procedures, and processes.  
    
* BSA/AML risk assessment.

* Customer Identification (CIP) implementation.  
    
* Adequacy of Customer Due Diligence (CDD) policies, procedures, and processes relative to the risk assessment.  
    
* Personnel adherence to the Bank’s BSA/AML/CIP policies, procedures, and processes.  
    
* Transaction testing with emphasis on high risk operations.  
    
* Training adequacy, including its comprehensiveness, accuracy of materials, training schedule, and attendance tracking.  
    
* The overall process for identifying and reporting suspicious activity, including a review of filed and prepared SARs to determine their accuracy, timeliness, completeness, and effectiveness of the bank’s policy.  
    
* The effectiveness of the suspicious monitoring systems used for BSA/AML compliance.  
* The integrity and accuracy of management information systems (MIS) used in the BSA/AML compliance program.  
    
* Management’s efforts to resolve violations and deficiencies noted in previous audits and regulatory examinations, including progress in addressing outstanding supervisory actions, if applicable.

**Internal Controls**

The Board of Directors, acting through senior management, is ultimately responsible for ensuring that the bank maintains effective BSA/AML controls. Internal controls are the banks policies, procedures, and processes designed to limit and control risks and to achieve compliance with the BSA.  The level of the controls should be appropriate to the size, structure, risks, and complexity of the bank.

* Trained Personnel:  All employees are trained to recognize reportable transactions and notify the proper authorities within the bank.  A high emphasis is placed on recognizing and reporting unusual activity. 

* Currency Transactions:  System generated reports are reviewed daily by the BSA or assistant BSA compliance officer to assist in the determination of CTR filing.  All tellers are instructed in obtaining the required reporting information. 

* Kiting reports are reviewed daily by the deposit operations department and /or the BSA compliance officer to detect suspect transactions with a goal to stop kiting in an early stage.  When a kite is determined or suspected, the teller staff is directed to place holds on specific deposited items to eliminate possible loss.  SARs are filed as necessary.

* High Risk Customers: The bank uses system generated reports, internal controls, and trained employees to mitigate the potential risk of any High- Risk account, product, or service being misused for illegal activity.  The BSA compliance officer reviews risk rating reports frequently; CSRs perform a risk assessment at account opening. A high-risk customer can also be identified or classified based on the totality of the circumstances, not just the type of business. High Risk customers may include regular customers, businesses, exempt customers, foreign customers or any other type of customers in the bank. All high-risk accounts will be closely monitored by the BSA Department.

* CIP Monitoring: The BSA compliance officer reviews all new accounts and risk rates them.  
    
* Review of Exempt Persons: The BSA compliance officer reviews the file for exempt persons at least annually and includes all verification documents.  The teller system also allows for exempt persons.  A report is generated to show all exempt accounts.  Account cash flows are reviewed.  
* Monetary Instruments: The BSA or assistant BSA compliance officer reviews the Official Document Report for monetary instruments periodically to ensure that all required information is present.

* Wire Transfer Logs: The BSA compliance officer reviews these logs periodically. 

* SARs: A review committee decides if a SAR should be filed.  All SARs are reported to the Board of Directors by the BSA compliance officer.

**Penalties for Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing, and Violations of BSA**

Penalties can be severe.  Banks and individuals may incur criminal or civil penalties or both for violating the laws.  

**Criminal**

A person convicted of money laundering can face up to 20 years in prison and a fine up to $500,000.  Any property involved in the transaction or traceable to the proceeds of the criminal may be subject to forfeiture.

The bank could lose its charter and bank employees could be removed or barred from banking.

A person, including a bank employee, willfully violating the BSA or its implementing regulations is subject to a criminal fine up to $250,000 or five years in prison or both.  A person who commits such a violation while violating another U.S. law, or engaging in a pattern of criminal activity, is subject to a fine up to $500,000 or ten years in prison or both.

A bank that violates certain BSA provisions can face criminal money penalties up to the greater of $1 million or twice the value of the transaction.

**Civil**

Federal banking agencies and FinCEN can bring civil money penalty actions for violations of the BSA.  Individuals may be removed from banking for a violation of the AML laws as long as the violation was not accidental.  All of these actions are available to the public.

**Currency Transaction Reporting**

The bank, along with all businesses, is required to report currency transactions exceeding $10,000. The purpose is to identify unusual cash flows of currency that may indicate illegal activity.

**Reporting Requirements**

A FinCEN[^1] Currency Transaction Report (CTR) will be filed electronically by the bank for each transaction in currency[^2], including foreign coin or currency exchanges, exceeding $10,000 by, through, or to the bank.

Reportable transactions of cash in or out include, but are not limited to:

* Deposits   
    
* Withdrawals  
    
* Purchased or cashed negotiable instruments  
    
* Currency Exchanges   
    
* Payments  
    
* Currency received or paid from funds transfers

**Transaction Aggregation**

Multiple currency transactions totaling more than $10,000 during any one business day[^3] are treated as a single transaction if the bank[^4] has knowledge[^5] that they are by or on behalf of the same person[^6].  Only “like[^7]” transactions, in terms of cash entries are subject to aggregation.

The bank has a computer system that allows it to aggregate multiple transactions to ensure that required CTRs are filed.

**Filing Time Frames and Record Retention**

The bank will file electronically with FinCEN within 15 calendar days after the date of the transaction.    
The bank will retain electronic copies of CTRs for five years from the date of the report.

**CTR Backfilling**	

The bank should begin filing CTRs on any reportable transactions it failed to file and contact the FinCEN Help Line at (800) 767-2825 or (703) 905-3591 or by emailing [FRC@fincen.gov](mailto:FRC@fincen.gov)  to request a back-filing determination.

**Exemptions** 

The purpose of exempting transactions from reporting is to eliminate the receipt of CTRs that serve no law enforcement purpose.  The bank may exempt certain types of customers from currency transaction reporting.  The Treasury published regulations in 1997 and 1998 that “reformed” the exemption process.  The concept of an “exempt person” was created and described the implementation of the changes as “Phase I” and “Phase II”.[^8]

The bank must electronically file a Designation of Exempt Person (DOEP) form to exempt a customer from CTR reporting.

**Phase I Exemptions**   
**Exempt Persons**:

* A bank, to the extent of its domestic operations[^9]  
    
* A federal, state, or local government agency or department  
    
* Any entity exercising governmental authority within the United States.  
    
* Any entity, other than a bank, whose common stock or analogous equity   
* interests[^10] are listed on the New York and American stock exchanges, or NASDAQ stock market (with some exceptions).  
    
*  Any subsidiary, other than a bank, of any “listed entity” that is organized under U.S. law and at least 51 percent of whose common stock or analogous equity interests is owned by the listed entity.

**Filing Time Frames**

The bank will electronically file a one-time Designation of Exempt Person form within 30 days after the first transaction in currency that the bank wishes to exempt.

**Monitoring**

The bank will perform an annual review to determine if a customer remains eligible for the Phase I exempt status.  Documentation, such as annual reports, stock quotes from newspapers or the Internet, will be maintained in the file to support eligibility.

**Phase II Exemptions**

An exempt person is an eligible non-listed business or payroll customer.

To be eligible for classification as a Phase II exempt person, a non-listed business must:

* Have frequent[^11](at least five in a 12-month period) currency transactions in excess of $10,000 or  
    
* Operate a firm that frequently withdrawals more than $10,000 to pay its U.S. employees in currency.  
* Be incorporated or organized under the laws of the United States or a state, or registered as and eligible to do business within the United States or state.  
    
* Maintain a transaction account at the bank for at least two months or after the bank conducts a risk based analysis of the legitimacy of the customer’s transactions.

Exempt persons are only exemptible to the extent of their transaction accounts including Money Market Deposit Accounts.

Some businesses are ineligible for treatment as an exempt non-listed business.  An ineligible business is defined as a business engaged primarily in one or more of the following activities:

* Serving as a financial institution or as agents for financial institution of any type  
    
* Practicing law, accounting, or medicine  
    
* Operating a pawn brokerage  
    
* Auctioning of goods  
* Purchasing or selling motor vehicles of any kind, vessels, aircraft, farm equipment or mobile homes  
    
* Chartering or operation of ships, buses or aircraft  
    
    
* Engaging in gaming of any kind (other than licensed pari-mutuel betting at race tracks)  
    
* Engaging in investment advisory services or investment banking services  
* Operating a real estate brokerage  
    
* Operating in title insurance services or real estate closings  
    
* Engaging in trade union activities  
    
* Engaging in any other activity specified by FinCEN, such as marijuana – related businesses

A business that engages in multiple business activities may be treated as a non-listed business as long as no more than 50% of its gross revenues per year are derived from one or more of the ineligible business activities listed above.

**Filing Time Frames**

The bank will electronically file a DOEP form within 30 days after the first transaction in currency that the bank wishes to exempt.

**Monitoring**

The bank will perform an annual review to determine if a customer remains eligible for the Phase II exempt status.  Information supporting eligibility will be maintained.  It will also review and verify that Phase II accounts are monitored for suspicious activity. The Bank is not required to record or report a change of control in a designated or payroll customer.

**Revocation of Exemption**

The bank may but is not required to file a DOEP form when an exemption is revoked.  The filing of CTRs provides adequate notice to the government that the bank no longer considers this customer to be an exempt person.

**Limitation of Liability of Failure to File CTRs**

No bank is liable for failure to file a report with respect to a currency transaction by an exempt person unless the bank:

* Knowingly provides false or incomplete information with respect to the transaction or the customer engaging in the transaction or;  
    
* Has reason to believe that the customer does not qualify as an exempt customer.

**Effect on Other Regulatory Requirements**

Exemption procedures do not change the requirements that the bank file SARs or its obligation to retain records of funds transfers by a person or in the connection of the sale of monetary instruments to a person.

**Simultaneous Purchases** 

Simultaneous purchases of the same or different types of instruments totaling $3,000 or more must be treated as one purchase. If there are aggregate sales but the bank does not know about them until after the sale, the bank must identify the sale as an aggregate sale.

If a person purchases a negotiable instrument for just under $3,000, but a service fee is added that brings the total to $3,000 or more, and the total amount is paid in currency, then the identification and recordkeeping requirements must be met.

**Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing**

**Money Laundering**

It is the criminal practice of processing ill-gotten gains, or “dirty’ money, through a series of transactions.  This is done so that the funds are “cleaned”; appear to be gotten from legal activities.  Currency doesn’t have to be involved in every stage of the money laundering process.  It is a diverse and often complex process.  There are three basic steps than can occur at the same time.

**Three Basic Steps**

* **Placement** – getting the money into a more convenient form.  This is the most vulnerable stage.  
* Structuring currency deposits in amounts to evade reporting requirements – dividing large amounts of currency into less-conspicuous smaller sums that are deposited directly into a bank account  
    
* Commingling currency deposits of legal and illegal enterprises

  EXAMPLE: Bubba S. is a small-time drug dealer.  His trade area is most of the rural county including seven school districts.  His clients are from 11 to 18 years old and pay him in singles, fives, tens and twenties.  His supplier insists on being paid weekly in large bills.

  The bills that Bubba receives are bulky and require more effort to conceal.  They also cannot be used to satisfy his most significant need for operating capital, the purchase of inventory.  Personally, he is at risk because everyone he deals with knows he carries large amounts of cash at all times.  He needs the cooperation of a financial institution to either perform currency exchanges or accept cash for deposit and allow withdrawals of large bills.

* **Layering** – conducting a series of transactions designed solely to make it difficult to trace the original source of funds.  It’s a more complex stage.

  EXAMPLE: Susan is the administrator of a money laundering business that handles approximately $300,000 weekly, mostly in cash, for a variety of illegal businesses.  For one client, she deposits the funds into 9 accounts for shell or fictitious companies, each at a different financial institution.  The money is wired to a bank in Atlanta for the credit of another fictitious company.  It is then wired to a non-cooperative country or territory (NCCT) and then to Spain or Columbia.  The Spanish depository wires the funds back to the US for deposit into the account of an apparently unrelated, shell company.  The Columbian depository 

* **Integration** – funds are used to acquire legitimate assets or fund further activities.  This is the ultimate stage.  It further shields the criminal from a recorded connection to the funds by providing a credible explanation of where the funds came from.

  EXAMPLE: “Lenny’s” is a pizza parlor in a major city.  Over a 12 month period its daily cash deposits are approximately $16,000. Elder Financial Exploitation

All employees need to report elder exploitation to the BSA Officer.

Older Americans hold a high concentration of wealth as compared to the general population. In the instances where elderly individuals experience declining cognitive or physical abilities, they may find themselves more reliant on specific individuals for their physical well-being, financial management, and social interaction. While anyone can be a victim of a financial crime such as identity theft, embezzlement, and fraudulent schemes, certain elderly individuals may be particularly vulnerable. 

The following red flags could indicate the existence of elder financial exploitation. This list of red flags identifies only *possible* signs of illicit activity. 

• Erratic or unusual banking transactions or changes in banking patterns: 

* Frequent large withdrawals, including daily maximum currency withdrawals from an ATM; 

* Sudden Non-Sufficient Fund activity; 

* Uncharacteristic nonpayment for services, which may indicate a loss of funds or access to funds; 

* Debit transactions that are inconsistent for the elder; 

* Uncharacteristic attempts to wire large sums of money; 

* Closing of CDs or accounts without regard to penalties. 

• Interactions with customers or caregivers:

* A caregiver or other individual shows excessive interest in the elder’s finances or assets, does not allow the elder to speak for himself, or is reluctant to leave the elder’s side during conversations; 

* The elder shows an unusual degree of fear or submissiveness toward a caregiver, or expresses a fear of eviction or nursing home placement if money is not given to a caretaker; 

* The financial institution is unable to speak directly with the elder, despite repeated attempts to contact him or her; 

* A new caretaker, relative, or friend suddenly begins conducting financial transactions on behalf of the elder without proper documentation; 

* The customer moves away from existing relationships and toward new associations with other “friends” or strangers; 

* The elderly individual’s financial management changes suddenly, such as through a change of power of attorney to a different family member or a new individual; 

* The elderly customer lacks knowledge about his or her financial status or shows a sudden reluctance to discuss financial matters.  
    
* If suspicious activity concerning elder abuse will file a SAR.

The Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) issued the Customer Due Diligence Requirements for Financial Institutions Rule (CDD Rule) in May 2016\. 

The CDD Rule requires us to establish procedures that enable us to identify the beneficial owners of each customer as the time a new account is opened, unless the customer is otherwise excluded or the transaction is exempted.  The procedures must establish risk-based practices verifying the identity of each beneficial owner identified, to the extent reasonable and practicable.  The procedures must contain the elements required for verifying the identities of customers that are individuals under applicable Customer Identification Program (CIP) requirements (i.e. We are required to obtain, verify and record the identity of the beneficial owners of legal entity customers.

The CDD Rule is applicable to “new” accounts.  The Rule defines a new account as each account opened as covered financial institution by a legal entity customer on or after May 11, 2018\.

The Rule does not supersede existing obligations and practices regarding trust.  While we are not required to look through a trust to its beneficiaries, we may need to take additional steps to verify the identity of a customer that is not an individual, such as obtaining information about the persons with control over the account.  When a trust is our direct customer, we will identify and verify the identity of trustees because trustees will be signatories on trust accounts.  Additionally, in the case of revocable trusts, we may need to gather information about the settlor, grantor, trustee or other persons with the authority to direct the trustee and who thus has the authority or control over the account, in order to establish the trust identity of the customer.

**Beneficial Owner-Defined**

The CDD defines a beneficial owner as each of the following

* Each individual, if any, who directly or indirectly, owns 25% or more of the equity interests of a legal entity customer (OWNERSHIP PRONG).  
* A single individual with significant responsibility to control, manage or direct a legal entity customer, including an executive officer or senior manager (e.g. CEO, CFO, COO, Managing Member, General Partner, President, Vice President or Treasurer); or any other individual who regularly performs similar functions (CONTROL PRONGS).

**Collection of Beneficial Ownership**

The CDD Rule requires us to obtain information about the beneficial owners of a legal entity from the individual seeking to open a new account on behalf of the legal entity customer.  This individual could, but would not necessarily, be a beneficial owner. Please refer to the **Beneficial Ownership Account Opening Procedures** to guide you through the account opening process and the **Customer Identification Program Procedures** for information required to be obtained from each beneficial owner (i.e. CIP procedures for individuals) and acceptable documentary and non-documentary methods of verifying an individual’s identity.

**OFAC Compliance**

We must use beneficial ownership information as we use other information we gather regarding customers, including compliance with OFAC-administered sanctions.  As such, we must screen each beneficial owner against the OFAC list prior to account opening and/or disbursement of loan proceeds.

**Section 314(a) Information Sharing**

FinCEN does not expect the information obtained under the CDD Rule to add additional 314(a) requirements.  The regulation implementing Section 314(a) does not require the reporting of beneficial ownership information associated with an account or transaction matching a named subject in a 314(a) request.  We are required to search our records for accounts or transactions matching a named subject and report whether a match exists using the identifying information provided in the request.  

**Exemptions and Limitations**

We are not required to identify and verify the identity of the beneficial owners of a legal entity customer when the customer opens any of the following

* Accounts established at the point-of-sale to provide credit products, solely for the purchase of retail goods and/or services at these retailers, up to a limit of $50,000.  
* Accounts established to finance the purchase of postage and for which payments are remitted directly by the FI to the provider of the postage products.  
* Accounts established to finance insurance premiums and for which payments are remitted directly by the FI to the insurance provider or broker.  
* Accounts established to finance the purchase or lease of equipment and for which payments are remitted directly by the FI to the vendor or lessor of the equipment.

These exemptions will **NOT** apply under **EITHER** of the following circumstances:

* If the accounts are transaction accounts through which a legal entity customer can make payments to, or receive payments from, third parties.  
* If there is the possibility of a cash refund for accounts opened to finance purchase of postage, insurance premium or equipment leasing.

Additionally, the Rule excludes from the definition of legal entity customer certain entities that are subject to Federal and State regulation and for which information about their beneficial ownership and management is available from the Federal and State agencies. The following list of exclusions have been outlined in the Rule

* Financial institutions regulated by a Federal functional regulator or a bank regulated by a State bank regulator  
* Department or agency of the U.S., of any State, or of any political subdivision of a State  
* Any entity established under the laws of the U.S., or any State, or any political subdivision of any State, or under an interstate compact  
* Any entity (other than a bank) whose common stock or analogous equity interests are listed on the New York, American, or NASDAQ stock exchange  
* Any entity organized under the laws of the U.S. or of any State at least 51% of whose common stock or analogous equity interests are held by a listed entity  
* Issuers of securities registered under Section 21 of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 or that is required to file reports under 15(d) of that Act  
* An SEC-registered investment adviser, as defined in Section 202(a)(11) of the investment Advisers Act of 1940  
* An exchange or clearing agency, as defined in Section 3 of the SEA, registered under Section 6 or 17A of that Act  
* Any other entity registered with the SEC under the SEA

* A public accounting firm registered under Section 102 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act

**Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) for Higher Risk Customers**

Enhanced due diligence should be performed prior to account opening and reviewed at least annually during the account relationship.

**Automated Clearing House Transactions (ACH) \-** an ACH transaction is a batch-processed, value-dated, electronic funds transfer between an originating and a receiving bank. An ACH credit transaction is originated by the accountholder sending funds (payer), while an ACH debit transaction is originated by the accountholder receiving funds (payee). Within the ACH system, these participants and users are known by the following terms: 

* Originator \- An organization or person that initiates an ACH transaction to an account either as a debit or credit

* Originating Depository Financial Institution (ODFI) \- The Originator’s depository financial institution that forwards the ACH transaction into the national ACH network through an ACH Operator

* ACH Operator \- processes all ACH transactions that flow between different depository financial institutions. An ACH Operator serves as a central clearing facility that receives entries from the ODFIs and distributes the entries to the appropriate Receiving Depository Financial Institution. There are currently two ACH Operators: FedACH and Electronic Payments Network (EPN)

* Receiving Depository Financial Institution (RDFI) \-The Receiver’s depository institution that receives the ACH transaction from the ACH Operators and credits or debits funds from their receivers’ accounts

* Receiver \- An organization or person that authorizes the Originator to initiate an ACH transaction, either as a debit or credit to an account

* Gateway Operator (GO) \- A financial institution, ACH Operator, or ODFI that acts as an entry or exit point to or from the United States. A formal declaration of status as a Gateway Operator is not required. ACH operators and ODFIs acting in the role of Gateway Operators have specific warranties and obligations related to certain international entries. A financial institution acting as a Gateway Operator generally may process inbound and outbound debit and credit transactions. ACH Operators acting as Gateway Operators may process outbound debit and credit entries, but can limit inbound entries to credit entries only and reversals. 

International ACH Payments (IAT) \- an ACH entry that is part of a payment transaction involving a financial agency’s office that is not located in the territorial jurisdiction of the United States. An office of a financial agency is involved in the payment transaction if one or more of the following conditions are met: 

Holds an account that is credited or debited as part of a payment transaction; or receives funds directly from a person or makes payment directly to a person as part of a payment transaction; or serves as an intermediary in the settlement of any part of a payment transaction. 

An “inbound entry” originates in another country and is transmitted to the United States. For example, an inbound entry could be funding for a company payroll. Each subsequent IAT used for direct deposit would be an inbound IAT entry. 

An “outbound entry” originates in the United States and is transmitted to another country. For example, IAT pension payments going from a U.S. ODFI to a U.S. RDFI in which the funds are then transferred to an account in another country would be outbound IAT entries.
