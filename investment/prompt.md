## 1. Purpose and Scope

This policy governs how Pynthia Credit Union invests surplus funds and places non-loan assets to protect safety and soundness, maintain adequate liquidity, earn a reasonable risk-adjusted yield, and comply with the Federal Credit Union Act and NCUA Part 703. It applies to all investments, repurchase and reverse repurchase agreements, deposits, and other non-loan asset placements held on the balance sheet, whether executed directly or through third parties. It does not authorize derivative or retail nondeposit investment product activity.

## 2. Key Regulatory Authorities

- **NCUA 12 CFR Part 703 (Investment and Deposit Activities, Subpart A)** — core authority for FCU investment and deposit activities, including written investment policies (§703.3), recordkeeping (§703.4), credit analysis (§703.6), broker-dealers (§703.8), safekeeping (§703.9), valuation (§703.11), monitoring (§703.12), permissible activities and investments (§703.13–703.14), and prohibited activities, investments, and conflicts of interest (§703.15–703.17).
- **NCUA 12 CFR §741.3** — insurability and reserve criteria, including the special reserve treatment of nonconforming investments for state charters.
- **NCUA 12 CFR §741.12** — liquidity and Contingency Funding Plan requirements by asset tier; the investment portfolio is a core contingent liquidity source.
- **NCUA repurchase guidance (IRPS 1985-2 and Part 703)** — safe-and-sound collateral practices, haircuts, and maturity-mismatch limits (e.g., 30-day baseline) for repurchase and reverse repurchase transactions.
- **NCUA Supervisory Letter on credit ratings (LCU 2013-05)** — expectations for independent internal credit analysis and limited reliance on NRSRO ratings under §703.6.
- **NCUA Examiner's Guide (Investment Analysis & Liquidity)** — expectations that the investment policy address credit, liquidity, interest rate, and concentration risk and integrate with ALM and liquidity risk management.

## 3. What This Policy Must Cover

Investment risk at Pynthia is concentrated in the credit, interest-rate, liquidity, and concentration exposures of the balance-sheet portfolio, in the integrity of trade execution and valuation, and in the governance discipline that keeps activity within board-approved authority and Part 703 limits.

The policy must establish the following controls:

**(a) Policy Objectives, Scope, and Covered Instruments.** Maintain a single canonical Investment Policy record with effective and next-review dates, and tag every balance-sheet position (investments, repos, deposits, other non-loan placements) that falls under it. The system warns 60 and 30 days before the next review; investments lapsing without board approval remain governed by the prior policy but are flagged non-compliant.

**(b) Governance, Board Oversight, and Delegations.** Maintain an authority matrix defining investment decision rights and single-trade and aggregate limits by role, link each trade and exception to an authorized approver, and require board review of the policy and authority matrix at least annually (not to exceed 12 months between approvals). Exceptions must be documented and approved before settlement.

**(c) Permissible Investments and Prohibited Activities.** Enforce an allow-list of instrument types mapped to Part 703 categories and block booking of prohibited or non-permitted instruments, reviewing the list at least annually and whenever Part 703 changes materially.

**(d) Interest Rate Risk and ALM Integration.** Capture effective duration, convexity, and cash-flow vectors per security and feed ALM models for IRR measurement, running ALCO IRR simulations at least quarterly (more often when thresholds are breached).

**(e) Credit Risk Standards and Downgrade Management.** Require a completed credit file and approval before booking eligible non-government investments, complete documented re-analysis at least annually, and review downgrades from the internal investment-grade threshold within 5 business days with board notification for material positions.

**(f) Liquidity and Marketability Limits.** Classify each investment by liquidity bucket, days-to-liquidate, and stress haircut, and report on-demand and 30-day stress liquidity capacity at least monthly (more frequently under stress).

**(g) Concentration and Counterparty Limits.** Maintain parameterized issuer, sector, rating, product, and counterparty limits with soft (warning) and hard (blocking) enforcement at trade entry, reviewed at least annually.

**(h) Approved Brokers, Dealers, and Safekeepers.** Maintain an approved intermediaries list with due-diligence and review dates, block trades with unapproved intermediaries, perform annual due-diligence reviews, and reconcile safekeeping statements monthly per §703.9(c).

**(i) Repurchase and Reverse Repurchase Agreements.** Represent each repo with explicit collateral, haircut, counterparty, and maturity-mismatch fields; block bookings that violate internal or regulatory mismatch and collateral rules; mark collateral to market (at least weekly, daily in stress) and issue margin calls on shortfalls.

**(j) Valuation, Accounting, and Fair-Value Measurement.** Capture book value, amortized cost, and fair value per security with pricing source and date, update fair value at least monthly, support OTTI analysis, and restrict pricing overrides to CFO/Controller under dual approval.

**(k) Pre-Purchase Due Diligence and Exceptions.** Enforce a pre-trade checklist and approval workflow linking each trade to a credit memo and valuation support before booking (other than U.S. government or fully insured instruments), with a board-thresholded exception log.

**(l) Ongoing Monitoring, Reporting, and Stress Testing.** Generate recurring composition, duration, liquidity, gain/loss, and limit-adherence reports (monthly to management, at least quarterly to the board) and run stress scenarios at least annually, more often as metrics approach limits.

**(m) Performance Measurement and Benchmarks.** Attribute return and income to benchmarks by segment quarterly and ensure performance targets do not incentivize breaching risk limits.

**(n) Trade Execution, Controls, and Segregation of Duties.** Segregate trade initiation, approval, confirmation, settlement, and accounting; enforce dual control and block any single user from controlling a full transaction lifecycle; reconcile within T+1.

**(o) Recordkeeping and Documentation Retention.** Store and index trade tickets, confirmations, credit memos, approvals, safekeeping statements, and reports with retention schedules; documents must be attached to trades within 2 business days.

**(p) Training, Competency, and Conflicts of Interest.** Track required training and annual COI certifications for covered staff and board members, requiring training before system access and auto-suspending access on non-completion.

**(q) Contingency Planning and Liquidity Stress Events.** Maintain predefined liquidity stress scenarios and an investment-liquidation hierarchy tied to the CFP, test at least annually, and document an initial execution plan within 1 business day of an actual stress declaration.

**(r) Policy Review, Amendments, and Version Control.** Maintain full version history, redlines, and approval metadata; review at least annually and earlier on material regulatory change.

Governance of these controls is centralized with the Chief Compliance Officer, with the Chief Investment Officer (or equivalent), ALCO/Investment Committee, Treasury, Risk, Finance, and the Internal Audit/Supervisory Committee as required participants.

## 4. Out of Scope

- Derivative authority under Part 703 Subpart B — requires a separate Derivatives Policy.
- Retail nondeposit investment product (NDIP) sales programs — covered by a separate NDIP/retail investment sales policy.
- Enterprise liquidity program and Contingency Funding Plan governance — see Liquidity Policy.
- Asset-liability and capital adequacy frameworks — see Capitalization and Basel-II Standardized Approach Framework Policies.
- Record retention schedules generally — see Record Retention Policy.
- Third-party/vendor risk management for brokers and custodians beyond investment due diligence — see Third-Party Risk Policy.
