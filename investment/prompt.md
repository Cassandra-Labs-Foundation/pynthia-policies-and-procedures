## 1. Purpose and Scope

This policy governs how Pynthia Credit Union invests surplus funds and places non-loan assets to protect safety and soundness, maintain adequate liquidity, earn a reasonable risk-adjusted yield, and comply with the Federal Credit Union Act and NCUA Part 703. It applies to all investments, repurchase and reverse repurchase agreements, deposits, and other non-loan asset placements held on the balance sheet, whether executed directly or through third parties. It does not authorize derivative or retail nondeposit investment product activity.

## 2. Key Regulatory Authorities

- **NCUA 12 CFR Part 703 (Investment and Deposit Activities, Subpart A)** — core authority for FCU investment and deposit activities, including written investment policies (§703.3), recordkeeping (§703.4), credit analysis (§703.6), broker-dealers (§703.8), safekeeping (§703.9), valuation (§703.11), monitoring (§703.12), permissible activities and investments (§703.13–703.14), and prohibited activities, investments, and conflicts of interest (§703.15–703.17).
- **NCUA 12 CFR §741.3** — insurability and reserve criteria, including the special reserve treatment of nonconforming investments for state charters.
- **NCUA 12 CFR §741.12** — liquidity and Contingency Funding Plan requirements by asset tier; the investment portfolio is a core contingent liquidity source.
- **NCUA repurchase guidance (IRPS 1985-2 and Part 703)** — safe-and-sound collateral practices, haircuts, and maturity-mismatch limits (e.g., 30-day baseline) for repurchase and reverse repurchase transactions.
- **NCUA Supervisory Letter on credit ratings (LCU 2013-05)** — expectations for independent internal credit analysis and limited reliance on NRSRO ratings under §703.6.
- **NCUA Examiner's Guide (Investment Analysis & Liquidity)** — expectations that the investment policy address credit, liquidity, interest rate, and concentration risk and integrate with ALM and liquidity risk management.

## 3. What This Policy Must Cover

Investment risk at Pynthia is concentrated in the credit, interest-rate, liquidity, and concentration exposures of the balance-sheet portfolio, in the integrity of trade execution and valuation, and in the governance discipline that keeps activity within board-approved authority and Part 703 limits.

The policy must establish the following controls:

**(a) Policy Objectives, Scope, and Covered Instruments.** Maintain a single canonical Investment Policy record with effective and next-review dates, and tag every balance-sheet position (investments, repos, deposits, other non-loan placements) that falls under it. The system warns 60 and 30 days before the next review; investments lapsing without board approval remain governed by the prior policy but are flagged non-compliant.

The policy must articulate the following investment objectives, all of which must be pursued consistent with safe and sound practices and NCUA Part 703:

1. **Provide Earnings.** Manage the portfolio to earn a reasonable risk-adjusted return that contributes to the credit union's financial goals and member value, through holding securities that individually and in combination produce superior risk/reward characteristics for the total portfolio.
2. **Provide Liquidity.** Maintain a portfolio sufficiently liquid to meet anticipated funding demands from declines in share deposits or increases in loan demand; investments must be marketable and convertible to cash at fair value in a reasonable time.
3. **Mitigate Interest Rate Risk.** Use portfolio strategies to assist in managing overall interest rate sensitivity in accordance with ALCO goals and objectives approved by the Board.
4. **Ensure Safety of Principal.** At all times safety of principal is the primary consideration; investments are limited to instruments permissible under Part 703 that comply with all applicable regulatory guidelines.
5. **Meet Pledging Requirements.** Use the portfolio to provide collateral for FHLB advances, public unit deposits, and other permissible funding arrangements.

**(b) Governance, Board Oversight, and Delegations.** Maintain an authority matrix defining investment decision rights and single-trade and aggregate limits by role, link each trade and exception to an authorized approver, and require board review of the policy and authority matrix at least annually (not to exceed 12 months between approvals). Exceptions must be documented and approved before settlement.

Board responsibilities include: (1) reviewing and approving the Investment Policy annually; (2) reviewing and approving the list of securities dealers; (3) reviewing and approving safekeeping agents; and (4) reviewing investment activity and summary reports at least quarterly (monthly preferred).

The ALCO/Investment Committee is responsible on at least a monthly basis for: developing investment strategies; monitoring investment activities and risk attributes; ensuring compliance with policy and regulatory requirements; reviewing economic conditions and interest rates; and reporting all investment activities to the Board.

The Chief Financial Officer (or designated Chief Investment Officer) serves as the primary investment officer responsible for day-to-day portfolio management, including buying and selling securities per approved policy, overseeing accounting and safekeeping, and ensuring all routine management duties are met. The CFO's aggregate purchase/sale authority within a calendar month may not exceed a limit set in the authority matrix; transactions beyond that limit require prior approval of the President/CEO or ALCO as specified in the matrix.

**(c) Permissible Investments and Prohibited Activities.** Enforce an allow-list of instrument types mapped to Part 703 §703.13–703.14 categories and block booking of prohibited or non-permitted instruments, reviewing the list at least annually and whenever Part 703 changes materially.

The portfolio must consist of high-quality investment securities and money market instruments permissible under NCUA Part 703. ALCO develops strategies by evaluating which combination of permissible instruments provides the best risk/reward profile for the total portfolio. Portfolio allocations are bounded by the maximum diversification guidelines below, expressed as a percentage of the total portfolio and as a percentage of Net Worth.

Instrument-level parameters (single security limit, single issuer limit, maximum maturity, and maximum weighted average life) for each permitted category must be documented in a permissible instruments appendix and reviewed annually. The following limits apply as a baseline:

- **U.S. Treasury obligations:** max maturity 15 years; single security limit 25% of Net Worth.
- **U.S. Agency direct debt:** max maturity 15 years; single security limit 25% of Net Worth; single issuer limit 200% of Net Worth.
- **Agency MBS:** max maturity 30 years; maximum weighted average life 20 years; single issuer limit 200% of Net Worth.
- **Agency CMOs:** max maturity 30 years; maximum weighted average life 20 years; single issuer limit 200% of Net Worth.
- **Agency HECMs:** max maturity 50 years; maximum weighted average life 20 years; single issuer limit 100% of Net Worth.
- **Structured/callable agency notes:** max maturity 20 years; maximum weighted average life 10 years; single issuer limit 100% of Net Worth.
- **Municipal GO bonds:** max maturity 30 years; max weighted average life 20 years; single security 15% of Net Worth; single issuer 25% of Net Worth.
- **Municipal essential purpose revenue bonds:** max maturity 30 years; max weighted average life 20 years; single security 15% of Net Worth; single issuer 25% of Net Worth.
- **Other municipal revenue bonds:** max maturity 15 years; max weighted average life 10 years; single security 15% of Net Worth; single issuer 25% of Net Worth.
- **Private-label MBS and ABS:** max maturity 30 years (MBS) / 15 years (ABS); max weighted average life 20 years (MBS) / 10 years (ABS); single security 15% of Net Worth.
- **Corporate bonds:** max maturity 20 years; single security 15% of Net Worth; single issuer 15% of Net Worth.
- **Overnight fed funds:** single issuer limit 100% of Net Worth (25% if counterparty is only adequately capitalized).
- **Term fed funds:** max maturity 90 days; same issuer limits as overnight.
- **NCUA-insured CDs:** max maturity 5 years; per-institution limit at fully insured/collateralized amount.
- **Repurchase agreements:** max maturity 3 months; single issuer 25% of Net Worth (see also control (i)).
- **Commercial paper:** max maturity 30 days; single issuer 25% of Net Worth.

The following are **prohibited** under NCUA Part 703 and must be blocked at trade entry: derivatives (absent a separate board-approved derivatives policy under Part 703 Subpart B); subordinated debt; non-investment-grade instruments (below the four highest NRSRO rating categories); stripped mortgage-backed securities unless specifically permitted; investments in instruments that would trigger §703.15 prohibited activities; and any instrument not on the approved allow-list.

**(d) Interest Rate Risk and ALM Integration.** Capture effective duration, convexity, and cash-flow vectors per security and feed ALM models for IRR measurement, running ALCO IRR simulations at least quarterly (more often when thresholds are breached).

The primary analytical tool for identifying superior risk/reward securities is prospective, scenario-based total return analysis. Total return analysis simulates income to a defined time horizon (current income) and then adds or subtracts the projected market value at that horizon — equivalent to combining an income simulation with an economic value of equity (EVE) calculation. This approach allows comparison of unlike cash flows using common denominators of time, rate scenarios, and assumptions. Management's role is not to predict interest rates but to evaluate what may happen to individual securities and the portfolio as a whole across plausible rate scenarios.

**Market Depreciation Limits.** The policy must establish specific limits for overall price depreciation of the combined investment portfolio (AFS and HTM). Given a parallel shift of the yield curve of +100, +200, and +300 basis points, the aggregate price depreciation of the investment portfolio may not exceed **30% of Net Worth**. This calculation must be performed no less frequently than quarterly and reported to ALCO and the Board.

**(e) Credit Risk Standards and Downgrade Management.** Require a completed credit file and approval before booking eligible non-government investments, complete documented re-analysis at least annually, and review downgrades from the internal investment-grade threshold within 5 business days with board notification for material positions.

For all investments not directly guaranteed by the U.S. government, the credit union may only purchase securities that meet the definition of **investment grade**: the issuer has adequate capacity to meet its financial commitments over the projected life of the investment, the risk of default is low, and full and timely repayment of principal and interest is expected. Per NCUA LCU 2013-05 and §703.6, management must perform independent internal credit analysis; NRSRO ratings may support but may not be the sole basis for investment-grade determination. On a general basis, the credit union will not purchase securities rated below the four highest NRSRO rating categories (see quality ratings appendix).

Per-instrument due diligence requirements are:

- **Municipal GO bonds:** confirm spreads to Treasuries are consistent with similar-quality bonds; confirm low default risk; conduct internal credit analysis of capacity to pay, operating and financial performance; assess local demographic and economic characteristics; evaluate soundness of budgetary position and stability of tax revenues.
- **Municipal essential purpose revenue bonds:** same as GO bonds plus evaluate sources and strength of the revenue structure; assess debt service coverage ratios and the issuer's ability to raise rates.
- **Other municipal revenue bonds:** same due diligence as essential purpose revenue bonds.
- **Private-label MBS and ABS:** confirm low default risk and capacity to pay; understand tranche position in securitization structure and cash-flow waterfall; understand loss allocation rules, default definitions, performance triggers, and credit/liquidity enhancements; evaluate underwriting quality of underlying collateral and any risk concentrations; determine whether current underwriting is consistent with historical performance; assess structural subordination adequacy; analyze impact of collateral deterioration on tranche performance under adverse conditions.
- **Corporate bonds:** confirm spreads to Treasuries consistent with similar quality; confirm low default risk; confirm capacity to pay through internal credit analysis of operating and financial performance.
- **Commercial paper and other short-term corporate obligations:** management must perform credit due diligence confirming investment-grade quality before entering into any arrangement.

**(f) Liquidity and Marketability Limits.** Classify each investment by liquidity bucket, days-to-liquidate, and stress haircut, and report on-demand and 30-day stress liquidity capacity at least monthly (more frequently under stress).

Liquidity risk is the risk that the credit union cannot sell, unwind, or offset a position at a fair price due to inadequate market depth. The portfolio must remain sufficiently liquid to meet all operating requirements that may be reasonably anticipated. This is accomplished by structuring the portfolio so that a sufficient amount of securities can be converted to cash; the portfolio must consist largely of securities with active secondary or resale markets. AFS securities may be sold prior to maturity to provide liquid funds as needed.

**(g) Concentration and Counterparty Limits.** Maintain parameterized issuer, sector, rating, product, and counterparty limits with soft (warning) and hard (blocking) enforcement at trade entry, reviewed at least annually. Concentration limits must address single or related issuer, geographic area, and obligations with similar cash flow or risk characteristics. The diversification limits table in control (c) constitutes the hard limits; ALCO may establish tighter operating limits by sector on a periodic basis.

**(h) Approved Brokers, Dealers, and Safekeepers.** Maintain an approved intermediaries list with due-diligence and review dates, block trades with unapproved intermediaries, perform annual due-diligence reviews, and reconcile safekeeping statements monthly per §703.9(c).

The Board reviews and approves the list of securities dealers at least annually. The credit union purchases and sells securities only through approved dealers. The CFO maintains current information on approved dealers. Dealer analysis must include: (1) the dealer's and its affiliates' ability to fulfill commitments as evidenced by capital strength, liquidity, and operating results; (2) the dealer's general reputation for financial stability and fair, honest dealings; (3) information from state or federal securities regulators and self-regulatory organizations such as FINRA concerning any formal enforcement actions against the dealer, its affiliates, or associated personnel; and (4) the experience and expertise of the sales representative.

Purchased securities must not be maintained with the selling dealer; they must be transferred to an approved safekeeping agent per §703.9. Bank records of investment safekeeping must be reconciled to reports from safekeeping agents at least monthly per §703.9(c). Approval of existing or additional safekeeping agents requires Board action.

**(i) Repurchase and Reverse Repurchase Agreements.** Represent each repo with explicit collateral, haircut, counterparty, and maturity-mismatch fields; block bookings that violate internal or regulatory mismatch and collateral rules; mark collateral to market (at least weekly, daily in stress) and issue margin calls on shortfalls.

Repurchase agreements are treated as secured borrowings: the credit union enters a simultaneous agreement to sell securities and repurchase them at a specified price on a specified future date, creating a liability on the balance sheet while the investment remains as an asset. Maximum maturity is 3 months as a baseline; maturity-mismatch limits apply per NCUA IRPS 1985-2 and Part 703 guidance. Single issuer limit is 25% of Net Worth.

**(j) Valuation, Accounting, and Fair-Value Measurement.** Capture book value, amortized cost, and fair value per security with pricing source and date, update fair value at least monthly, support OTTI analysis, and restrict pricing overrides to CFO/Controller under dual approval.

At the time of purchase, all securities must be classified as Held-to-Maturity (HTM) or Available-for-Sale (AFS) in accordance with GAAP and regulatory requirements:

- **HTM securities:** debt securities for which the credit union has the positive intent and ability to hold to maturity; reported at amortized cost.
- **AFS securities:** debt securities not classified as HTM; reported at fair value with unrealized gains and losses excluded from income and reported in a separate component of members' equity on a tax-affected basis until realized.

Transfers between categories are rare and must be clearly documented by the CFO. Items may be sold or transferred out of HTM only for: (1) less than 3 months to maturity or effective call date; (2) less than 15% of purchase face remaining on MBS/CMO; (3) deterioration of creditworthiness; (4) major regulatory change; or (5) a business combination or disposition resulting in an unacceptable asset/liability position.

The portfolio may be repositioned (AFS securities sold) when warranted to: improve the portfolio's risk/reward profile in response to market changes; adjust interest rate sensitivity; improve liquidity; or improve credit quality.

**Fair Value Measurement.** At least quarterly, the credit union must verify that reported fair values are reasonable and supported by reputable, independent pricing sources. Fair value is the price that would be received to sell an asset in an orderly transaction between market participants at the measurement date.

**OTTI.** Management must conduct a quarterly security-by-security evaluation to determine whether other-than-temporary impairment (OTTI) must be recorded. For securities in a loss position of 10% or more, management must assess whether the issuer is unable to pay all contractual amounts due; if probable, OTTI is recorded and the security is written down to fair value with the credit impairment flowing through income.

**(k) Pre-Purchase Due Diligence and Exceptions.** Enforce a pre-trade checklist and approval workflow linking each trade to a credit memo and valuation support before booking (other than U.S. government or fully insured instruments), with a board-thresholded exception log.

Required documentation for each **purchase transaction** must include: bond issuer; bond/security type; CUSIP number; issue size; issue date, maturity date, call date; coupon and coupon frequency; trade date and settlement date; par value, original issue price, and credit union purchase price; prospective total return profile; yield, duration, and weighted average life; CPR/PSA assumptions and analysis (if applicable); credit analysis memo (required for non-government instruments); AFS vs. HTM classification designation; and name of dealer.

Required documentation for each **sale transaction** must include: bond issuer; bond/security type; CUSIP number; rationale for sale; total return profile at sale price; trade date; settlement date; coupon; price; yield; par value; and name of dealer.

**(l) Ongoing Monitoring, Reporting, and Stress Testing.** Generate recurring composition, duration, liquidity, gain/loss, and limit-adherence reports (monthly to management, at least quarterly to the board) and run stress scenarios at least annually, more often as metrics approach limits.

The Board receives at least quarterly (monthly preferred): all security purchases; all security sales and net gains/(losses); portfolio composition; prospective total return profile; portfolio total return results; portfolio yield, current effective duration, and current average life; credit risk considerations; market appreciation or depreciation of the portfolio; and market appreciation/depreciation as a percent of Net Worth.

ALCO receives at least monthly: current and desired portfolio composition; prospective total return profile over one or more time horizons; analysis of risk considerations; liquidity objectives; portfolio total return results, current effective duration, and current average life; and credit union net worth levels.

**(m) Performance Measurement and Benchmarks.** Attribute return and income to benchmarks by segment quarterly and ensure performance targets do not incentivize breaching risk limits. Total return analysis — combining income simulation with projected market value at the horizon — is the primary tool for evaluating performance and for comparing unlike cash flows across rate scenarios. Performance measurement must support ALCO's assessment of whether the portfolio's risk/reward profile is consistent with approved strategy.

**(n) Trade Execution, Controls, and Segregation of Duties.** Segregate trade initiation, approval, confirmation, settlement, and accounting; enforce dual control and block any single user from controlling a full transaction lifecycle; reconcile within T+1.

**(o) Recordkeeping and Documentation Retention.** Store and index trade tickets, confirmations, credit memos, approvals, safekeeping statements, and reports with retention schedules; documents must be attached to trades within 2 business days. Retention schedules must comply with applicable NCUA and federal record retention requirements.

**(p) Training, Competency, and Conflicts of Interest.** Track required training and annual COI certifications for covered staff and board members, requiring training before system access and auto-suspending access on non-completion.

**(q) Contingency Planning and Liquidity Stress Events.** Maintain predefined liquidity stress scenarios and an investment-liquidation hierarchy tied to the Contingency Funding Plan (CFP) per §741.12, test at least annually, and document an initial execution plan within 1 business day of an actual stress declaration. The portfolio must be structured so that a sufficient tranche of AFS securities can be liquidated under stress to meet contingent funding needs without resorting to fire-sale pricing.

**(r) Policy Review, Amendments, and Version Control.** Maintain full version history, redlines, and approval metadata; review at least annually (not to exceed 12 months between board approvals) and earlier on material regulatory change to Part 703 or related guidance.

Governance of these controls is centralized with the Chief Compliance Officer, with the Chief Financial Officer/Chief Investment Officer (or equivalent), ALCO/Investment Committee, Treasury, Risk, Finance, and the Internal Audit/Supervisory Committee as required participants.

## 4. Out of Scope

- Derivative authority under Part 703 Subpart B — requires a separate Derivatives Policy.
- Retail nondeposit investment product (NDIP) sales programs — covered by a separate NDIP/retail investment sales policy.
- Enterprise liquidity program and Contingency Funding Plan governance — see Liquidity Policy.
- Asset-liability and capital adequacy frameworks — see Capitalization and Basel-II Standardized Approach Framework Policies.
- Record retention schedules generally — see Record Retention Policy.
- Third-party/vendor risk management for brokers and custodians beyond investment due diligence — see Third-Party Risk Policy.
