**Detecting Red Flags**  
Existing policies and procedures, including but not limited to CIP and lending, shall be followed and used to detect red flags. When any of the above-identified red flags, or any other red flags, are detected in connection with opening an account or maintaining an existing account, the policies and procedures for responding to red flags shall be followed.

**Responding to Red Flags**  
The chart lists possible responses to the red flags, depending on the type of account offered and the red flag.  When a potential red flag is detected, staff needs to complete the appropriate form.  If the red flag is on a credit report, the “Red Flag Credit Report Alert Form” is used.  If the red flag is on an existing account, when opening a new account, or when taking an application for loan or service, the “Red Flag Alert Form” is used.  The appropriate response will depend on the circumstances.  Staff may determine that none of the possible responses are necessary and conclude that no response is warranted.  If no response is needed, the form must still be completed and mark selection for no response (Red Flag Alert Form) or document no response needed (Red Flag Credit Report Alert Form).  All completed forms will be given to the ID Theft Compliance Officer.

All completed forms will be kept in the “Red Flag Alert” book (located in Rachel’s office).  Any significant incidents and the credit union’s response shall be documented in the log in the front of the “Red Flag Alert” book.  The credit union’s ID Theft Compliance Officer shall determine what incidents warrant documentation in the log.  The log should only contain incidents that are likely to or did have a major effect on the member or the credit union (and are greater than $3000).  The log should provide the Board with a meaningful compilation of significant red flags.  
   
**Updating the Identity Theft Red Flag Program**  
The credit union will update the Identity Theft Red Flag program periodically to reflect changes in risk to members or the safety and soundness of the credit union.  Such changes may include, but are not limited to, changes in methods of identity theft; changes in methods to detect, prevent, and mitigate identity theft; changes in types of accounts that the credit union offers or maintains; and changes in the business arrangements of the credit union including mergers, acquisitions, alliances, joint ventures, and service provider arrangements.

**Administering the Program**  
The Board will approve the initial written Program and will provide oversight for administering the Program.  The Board’s oversight will include: 1\) Designating the staff member responsible for implementation, known as “ID Theft Compliance Officer”; 2\) Reviewing annual compliance report (which includes the log); and 3\) approving material changes to the program.

The ID Theft Compliance Officer will ensure training for the program is completed by all staff annually or more frequently, if needed, and will document such training.  The Program training will be incorporated into new staff training as well.

The ID Theft Compliance Officer will oversee preparation of the annual compliance report to the Board.  The compliance report shall include:

1) The effectiveness of the Program;  
   2) Any service provider arrangements in regards to the Program;  
      3) Any significant incidents involving identity theft and the credit union’s response; and  
      4) Recommendations for material changes to the Program, if any.

The ID Theft Compliance Officer must ensure that the credit union exercises appropriate and effective oversight of service providers.  Service provider contracts shall require the service provider to have their own policies and procedures in place to detect red flags and either report red flags to the credit union or take appropriate steps to prevent or mitigate identity theft.  “Service provider” includes any entity that obtains a member’s “nonpublic personal information” (as defined by NCUA Rules & Regulations Part 716\) from the credit union pursuant to a contract for services.  Example would be Credit Information Systems (use to pull credit reports).
