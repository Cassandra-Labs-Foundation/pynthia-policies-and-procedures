## Overview

Pynthia Credit Union maintains a comprehensive record-retention and destruction program that establishes minimum retention periods by record class, governs the systematic and defensible disposal of obsolete records, and ensures that records can be promptly produced for examiners, auditors, and legal proceedings. Retention risk concentrates at both extremes: destroying records too early undermines the ability to demonstrate compliance or defend a claim, while retaining them too long creates security exposure, storage cost, and deletion-rights obligations under California's consumer privacy law. Pynthia's program addresses this tension by assigning clear ownership, maintaining a product-appropriate retention schedule keyed to its actual deposit, lending, and payment activities, governing certified destruction with two-person verification, and layering privacy-protective controls — including anonymization of stale BSA/AML data and systematic refresh of customer due diligence records — on top of the federal and California minimums. Because Pynthia is a California-chartered, federally insured credit union, both California Financial Code requirements and federal retention obligations apply, and the program resolves conflicts by applying whichever period is longer.

## Regulatory Framework

Pynthia's record-retention obligations flow from a layered framework of federal and California authorities, each anchored to specific record classes. The Bank Secrecy Act and its implementing regulations require five-year retention of BSA/AML records — transaction reports, suspicious-activity reports, currency-transaction reports, monetary-instrument logs, and customer due diligence documentation — reflecting Congress's judgment that financial-crime records must remain available for law-enforcement investigations and regulatory examinations well after the underlying transactions settle.

Federal consumer-protection regulations impose separate minimum retention periods tied to specific disclosure and decision events. Regulation B's adverse-action and credit-evaluation records must be retained for 25 months from the date of the action. Regulation Z requires retention of disclosure evidence for 3 years after the relevant disclosure. Regulation CC and Regulation E each require 2-year retention of compliance evidence. HMDA's Regulation C requires retention of the loan/application register. Because administering multiple short windows across the same records increases error risk, Pynthia applies a unified 3-year retention schedule to consumer transaction records subject to the 2-year and 25-month minimums; the additional retention period is justified by consistent administration and does not materially increase exposure under California's consumer privacy law.

The Interagency Guidelines Establishing Information Security Standards govern the security, handling, and disposal of customer-information records. Those guidelines require administrative safeguards — including a written program, accountable ownership, employee training, and certified destruction by a licensed vendor — to protect customer records throughout their life cycle. Pynthia implements these requirements through its destruction procedures, two-person verification, and vendor-certificate retention.

California's consumer privacy framework adds a data-minimization dimension that federal retention rules do not directly address. Once the purpose for which personal information was collected has been served and the applicable retention period has expired, the California Consumer Privacy Rights Act creates an obligation to destroy or de-identify that information. Pynthia's program directly integrates this obligation into the BSA/AML anonymization control and the CDD refresh cycle, ensuring that individually identifiable records are not carried forward beyond the point where retention is justified.

The GLBA and FCRA impose additional safeguards governing the security of customer information and access to consumer reports, respectively. Where those authorities impose retention obligations, they are incorporated into Schedule A. IRS tax-record rules, wire-transfer recordkeeping requirements, and other federal obligations are likewise reflected in Schedule A's record-class entries.

## Responsibility and Administration {#responsibility-and-administration}

Pynthia designates a single senior owner — at the SVP of Operations and Finance level — responsible for monitoring the credit union's retention and destruction activity and reporting to the Board. That owner maintains this policy and Schedule A, tracks compliance across departments, and ensures that records are retained and destroyed according to the schedule. Each department names its own records-retention contact, who is accountable for proper completion of retention within that department, develops department-specific training, and coordinates with the program owner on schedule updates.

Accountability is kept current by recording each designation and opening a re-designation task whenever a program-owner or departmental-contact role becomes vacant. A vacancy that is not filled before the next destruction or review cycle represents a gap in the program's chain of custody and is treated as an operational alert requiring prompt remediation. The program owner reports program status to the Board on the governance cycle, so the Board has ongoing visibility into whether retention and destruction are being managed appropriately.

## Retention Schedule — Schedule A {#retention-schedule-schedule-a}

Schedule A is the operational heart of the record-retention program. It is a versioned mapping of record class to minimum retention period, keyed to Pynthia's actual deposit, lending, and payment products — not to the Alabama Banking Code schedules carried in the reference policy, which are expressly excluded. Every record created in Pynthia's systems is assigned a Schedule A class and a retention anchor date on the day it is created; the retention clock runs from that anchor, and the record becomes disposal-eligible only after the documented retention period expires.

Federal minimums govern each class: five years for BSA/AML records; three years for Regulation Z disclosures; a unified three-year schedule for consumer records subject to the two-year Regulation CC and Regulation E periods and the 25-month Regulation B period; and HMDA register retention for the loan/application register. California Financial Code overlays apply where state law imposes a longer period. Records that are substantially similar to a scheduled item inherit the appropriate period. Records that cannot be matched to any class are flagged for Compliance to disposition rather than silently defaulted, because a silent default in either direction — too short or too long — undermines the program's defensibility.

Schedule A is reviewed and versioned at least annually, and an interim review is triggered whenever a monitored regulation changes a retention requirement. The program owner and departmental contacts share responsibility for keeping the schedule current; proposed changes flow from the departments to the program owner, who publishes the updated version.

## Retention Methods and Electronic-Record Integrity {#retention-methods-and-electronic-record-integrity}

Pynthia permits records to be retained in any reproducible form — hard copy, imaging, microform, or electronic media — as long as electronic records remain accurate, accessible, and of sufficient integrity for examiners to determine the credit union's financial condition and the substance and purpose of its transactions. This standard, drawn from the Interagency Guidelines, is not aspirational: records that cannot be reproduced on demand fail the test, regardless of the storage medium.

The program monitors electronic-record integrity through periodic sampling: a representative set of electronic records is reproduced on a recurring cycle, and any reproduction that fails the accuracy or accessibility test is logged and routed for remediation. When records are converted from one medium to another — for example, when paper records are scanned and stored digitally — the conversion is verified and certified so the reproduced record carries the same retention status as the original. IT and Compliance jointly own integrity-test configuration and conversion certification, and both functions are responsible for escalating failures before they accumulate into systemic gaps.

## Annual Policy and Schedule Review {#annual-policy-and-schedule-review}

This policy and Schedule A are reviewed at least annually. The review considers changes in state and federal reporting requirements, changes in Pynthia's products and services, changes in operational complexity, changes in consumer-compliance obligations, technological advances, and changes in storage costs. Annual review is the mechanism by which the program adapts to a regulatory environment that does not stand still; skipping a review risks both over-retention (continuing to hold records past a shortened period) and under-retention (failing to extend periods that have been lengthened by new rules).

Departmental contacts review their Schedule A entries and propose updates to the program owner. Where a monitored regulation changes a retention requirement, the program does not wait for the annual cycle — an interim review is triggered promptly. The published policy and Schedule A versions are locked following each review, and the program owner reports the completion of the review to the Board as part of the governance reporting cycle.

## Defensible, Certified Destruction {#defensible-certified-destruction}

Records are destroyed only after the documented destruction date, using a licensed destruction vendor, with two-person verification, and with the vendor's destruction certificate retained and attached to the destruction log entry. Each of these requirements is independently load-bearing: the destruction date requirement ensures that records are not destroyed prematurely; the licensed-vendor requirement ensures that the physical or electronic destruction actually eliminates the records; the two-person verification requirement prevents unilateral or unauthorized destruction; and the certificate creates the documentary evidence that destruction occurred when and how it was supposed to.

Records subject to a legal hold are excluded from disposal eligibility until the hold is formally released, regardless of where their retention timer stands. This is not optional: a record destroyed while subject to a legal hold constitutes spoliation and can expose the credit union to sanctions in litigation. The system enforces this exclusion automatically; an attempt to execute destruction on a held record is blocked and logged.

## Destruction Logging and Storage Convention {#destruction-logging-and-storage-convention}

Each department maintains a Destruction Log — Exhibit 1 to this policy — for examiner review. Every storage box of physical records is labeled with four minimum data elements: Box Number, Content Description, From and To Dates, and Destruction Date. These elements must match the corresponding log entry; a discrepancy between the box label and the log entry is flagged and must be resolved before destruction proceeds.

The logging and labeling convention is not administrative formality. It is what makes the program auditable: an examiner who wants to verify that a particular set of records was held for the required period and destroyed on the documented date can do so by tracing from the schedule to the log to the box label to the vendor certificate. A program with weak logging cannot demonstrate that destruction was lawful even if it was; a program with incomplete labeling cannot be sampled reliably during examinations.

## Legal Holds {#legal-holds}

When litigation, an investigation, or a subpoena arises, a legal hold is placed immediately. The hold suspends the retention clock and blocks disposal for every record within the hold's scope; held records cannot become disposal-eligible regardless of their retention timer's status. The hold remains in place until authorized counsel formally releases it, at which point the retention clock resumes from where it was suspended and normal disposal eligibility is restored.

Legal-hold placement and release are write-restricted to Legal and Compliance. The program tracks open holds, days-to-release after matter closure, and any disposal attempt blocked by a hold. An attempt to destroy a held record is treated as an alert requiring immediate investigation, not a routine exception.

## Core Processor and Email Archives {#core-processor-and-email-archives}

A substantial portion of Pynthia's critical member and transaction records are maintained on the core processor's electronic archive rather than in separately managed files. Each department is accountable for confirming that the proper retention timeframes have been configured for the records it maintains on that archive; the system records that confirmation against the archive's retention configuration, and any configuration gap is treated as a compliance finding.

Email archives are owned by IT. Proper retention timeframes for email are tested periodically to confirm that archived emails remain retrievable. Failed retrievals are logged and escalated for remediation. The distinction between core-processor records and email archives matters because the two have different governance paths — departmental contacts own the former, IT owns the latter — but both must meet the same accuracy, accessibility, and integrity standards that govern records in any other medium.

## Records Retention Training {#records-retention-training}

Annual record-retention training is provided to all employees. Each department's records-retention contact develops department-specific content covering this policy and that department's particular retention requirements. New hires in covered roles receive training as part of onboarding. Lapsed or incomplete training triggers a remedial assignment.

Training is not a formality. Staff who do not understand retention and destruction requirements make errors that the program cannot detect until an examination or a litigation hold surfaces a missing record or an improperly destroyed one. The program tracks completion rates by department and flags lapsed assignments before the annual cycle closes.

## BSA/AML Anonymization and Extended Analytical Retention {#bsaml-anonymization-and-extended-analytical-retention}

The Bank Secrecy Act requires Pynthia to retain BSA/AML records for five years. After that period expires, the California Consumer Privacy Rights Act's data-minimization principle requires the credit union to destroy or de-identify individually identifiable customer information — retaining it beyond its retention purpose without legal basis exposes Pynthia to deletion-rights claims and regulatory scrutiny.

Pynthia's approach distinguishes between two categories of post-five-year BSA/AML data. Individually identifiable information that has served its retention purpose must be destroyed under the standard destruction procedure or anonymized to CPRA de-identification standards — meaning that reasonable measures have been taken to prevent re-identification and that Pynthia has publicly committed not to re-identify the data. Anonymized and aggregated data from which no individual can be identified may be retained beyond five years for compliance analytics, trend analysis, and program improvement, because data in that form no longer constitutes personal information subject to the CPRA's deletion obligations.

The critical operational requirement is the audit trail: the system records when anonymization occurred, what method was applied, and that re-identification safeguards are in place, so that examiners can confirm the disposition of individually identifiable records even when older analytical data cannot be traced to specific members. Every BSA/AML record that reaches the five-year mark without a documented disposition — either a destruction record or an anonymization certificate — triggers an alert requiring resolution before the record ages further.

## CDD Refresh Cycle and Stale-Record Disposition {#cdd-refresh-cycle-and-stale-record-disposition}

Customer Due Diligence records are refreshed on a rolling basis tied to customer risk: at minimum annually for higher-risk customers, every two years for standard-risk customers, and immediately upon any new product or service added to a customer relationship. A rolling refresh cycle, as opposed to a single-event CDD review, is necessary because customer risk profiles change over time, and a profile that was accurate at account opening may be stale and misleading years later.

When a refresh is completed, superseded CDD records — information that has been updated or replaced by the refresh — are evaluated and disposed of rather than carried forward. Carrying forward stale personal information that is no longer accurate or needed is exactly what the California Consumer Privacy Rights Act's data-minimization principle is designed to prevent, and it also degrades BSA program quality by allowing outdated risk assessments to persist alongside current ones. Disposition follows the standard destruction procedure or the anonymization procedure in the BSA/AML anonymization control, depending on whether the superseded information can be de-identified and retained for analytical purposes.

The BSA department documents the refresh date, the triggering event — whether a scheduled periodic review or the addition of a new product or service — and the disposition of superseded records in the member file. This documentation supports both BSA examination readiness (examiners can verify that risk profiles are current) and CPRA compliance (the credit union can demonstrate that stale personal information was systematically eliminated rather than accumulated).

## Governance and Accountability {#governance-and-accountability}

The Chief Compliance Officer owns this policy and is accountable for the program overall. Day-to-day administration is delegated to the SVP of Operations and Finance, who monitors retention and destruction activity across departments and reports to the Board. Each department's records-retention contact is accountable for proper completion of retention within that department and is the first-line reviewer of Schedule A entries for that department's records.

Required participants beyond the program owner and departmental contacts include IT (for email-archive testing and core-processor retention configuration) and Internal Audit (for independent verification that the program is operating as designed). Material changes to this policy or Schedule A require re-approval by the Chief Compliance Officer and are reported to the Board.

This policy is reviewed at least annually. An out-of-cycle review is triggered when a monitored regulation changes a retention requirement, when a new product or service is introduced that requires new Schedule A entries, or when an examination finding identifies a gap in the program. Adjacent policies include the BSA Policy (for the substantive AML program whose recordkeeping this policy governs), the Cash Policy and Lending Policy (for deposit and loan records management beyond retention periods), the Information Security Policy (for security safeguards over records and email systems), and the Privacy Policy (for consumer deletion rights).

## Open Items {#open-items}

Several dependencies on engineering and operations require confirmation before the full program is operational. The record-management, schedule, destruction-log, legal-hold, core-archive, email-archive, and training events and timers referenced in the control overlays draw on the registered vocabulary in the engineering specification, but specific timer intervals — particularly for electronic-record integrity testing and core-processor retention confirmation cycles — have not been fixed; IT and Compliance need to confirm the appropriate cadence and register the corresponding timers.

The California Financial Code overlay specifics need confirmation by Legal and Compliance. The policy assumes federal minimums govern unless a California provision is longer, but the specific record classes where California law extends the federal period need to be enumerated in Schedule A before the schedule is treated as complete.

The charter and examiner applicability assumptions require verification. This policy frames its safeguard obligations around the Interagency Guidelines as they apply to federally insured credit unions, but the specific examining authority's expectations regarding disposal procedures and third-party vendor certifications should be confirmed and reconciled.

Employee and HR personnel records have no dedicated HR policy in this repository. Whether those records are governed by this program or deferred to a future HR policy is an open question; they are not separately controlled here beyond general record-class handling under Schedule A.
